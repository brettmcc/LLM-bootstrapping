\begin{abstract}
Reported standard errors capture sampling uncertainty conditional on one set of researcher decisions, but defensible alternatives in sample construction and specification can shift estimates substantially. We use repeated, independent LLM runs as stochastic replication agents---each receiving the same prompt and dataset but generating different research choices---to measure this researcher-induced variation. Applying the method to the immigration policy--employment question studied by \citet{huntingtonklein2025sources}, we generate 139 AI-written specifications with an interquartile range of $[-0.042,\, 0.046]$. Our analysis supports AI agents as scalable proxies for multi-analyst designs to characterize researcher-induced estimation uncertainty.

\medskip
\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} Researcher degrees of freedom, replication, specification uncertainty, Large Language Models

\medskip
\noindent \textbf{JEL Codes:} C12, C15, C18
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Empirical papers routinely report standard errors that quantify sampling uncertainty. These standard errors are conditional on a set of researcher decisions: which dataset to analyze, how to define the estimation sample, how to construct outcomes and treatments, and which specification and standard-error adjustments to use. A growing body of evidence shows that researchers making different  decisions can produce meaningfully different estimates from the same underlying data \citep{silberzahn2018many, huntington2021influence, breznau2022observing}. This implies an additional source of uncertainty beyond standard errors capturing sampling variation: researcher degrees of freedom.

Measuring parameter uncertainty due to researcher degrees of freedom at scale is challenging. Multi-analyst studies are expensive, requiring extensive recruitment and coordination. Pre-analysis plans constrain discretion but do not measure the sensitivity of results to alternative, still-defensible research choices \citep{olken2015promises}. Specification-curve and multiverse analyses require the original analyst to enumerate and implement various alternatives \citep{simonsohn2020specification, steegen2016multiverse}.

We overcome these limitations using a novel approach: generative AI agents can perform empirical analyses at scale. We apply this approach to the DACA--employment question studied by many analysts in \citet{huntingtonklein2025sources}: how does eligibility for the Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals program affect the probability of full-time employment?
Across 139 AI-generated specifications, we obtain an interquartile range from $-0.042$ to 0.046. Sample sizes vary substantially (IQR 53{,}232 to 125{,}521). This combination---near-zero average point estimate, a wide estimate distribution, and sizable sample-size heterogeneity---broadly resembles the patterns from the benchmark multi-analyst study, in which human researchers produce an IQR from 0.014 to 0.051 under comparable high-discretion conditions.

Two distinctions are essential for interpretation. First, the distribution of executed estimates is not a sampling distribution: because all runs use the same realized dataset, dispersion reflects heterogeneity in research choices, not classical sampling error. Second, different research choices can create \emph{estimand mismatch}---two defensible specifications may implicitly target different causal parameters by changing the treated population, timing window, or treatment definition \citep{lundberg2021estimand}.

We make two main contributions. First, we provide an open-source, scalable approach towards multi-analyst designs, allowing researchers to quantify researcher-induced uncertainty. Second, we show that repeated LLM runs provide a computationally tractable way to approximate the distribution of human research choices at scale: each run generates an interpretable, machine-readable specification that can be executed without human intervention.

\paragraph{Related literature.}
Our work builds on the researcher degrees of freedom literature, which documents how defensible analytic choices affect results \citep{simmons2011false, gelman2013garden, steegen2016multiverse, simonsohn2020specification}. Relative to analyst-enumerated multiverses, we introduce AI-enumerated multiverses across the entire research pipeline, allowing for quantification of analyst-induced estimate uncertainty at scale.

Repeated LLM runs play a role analogous to bootstrap resampling \citep{efron1979bootstrap, andrews2000bootstrap}, approximating a distribution through repeated draws---here over research choices rather than data. We also connect to emerging work on LLMs as economic agents and research tools \citep{horton2023large, brand2023gpt, korinek2023generative, novymarx2026ai}.

Section~\ref{sec:framework} formalizes the research-choice space and the LLM-induced distribution. Section~\ref{sec:software} documents the AI workflow. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports the distribution of executed estimates, compares it to the human-analyst benchmark, and relates heterogeneity both to specification features and to AI model choice. The conclusion discusses the main limitations and implications.

\section{Framework: researcher-induced uncertainty}
\label{sec:framework}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We formalize the objects of interest in two steps. First, we define the research problem, the target estimand, and the set of defensible research choices. We then treat repeated LLM runs as a stochastic mechanism that samples from that set.

\subsection{Scientific target and defensible research choices}
\label{sec:framework_target}

A research question asks how treatment $T$ affects outcome $Y$ in a given population $U$. Define a research problem $P$ as a tuple of defensible choice sets,
\[
P = (\mathcal{U},\mathcal{Y},\mathcal{T},\mathcal{S}),
\]
where $\mathcal{U}$ is the set of defensible sample definitions, $\mathcal{Y}$ is the set of defensible outcome constructions, $\mathcal{T}$ is the set of defensible treatment constructions, and $\mathcal{S}$ is the set of defensible specifications. An element of $\mathcal{S}$ collects the control set, functional form, estimator, weighting, fixed effects, and variance-estimation choices such as clustering.

For example, in our empirical application we consider the effect of the immigration policy, Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals (DACA), on the likelihood of full-time work among affected immigrants. Researchers may make different defensible choices about age bounds, full-time work definitions, eligibility proxies, controls, and standard-error adjustments. Different combinations of these choices imply different research designs and, in some cases, different causal parameters.

Not every logically possible combination is defensible. A sample definition that includes only U.S. citizens would be inconsistent with the research question which asks about impacts on affected \textit{immigrants}, whereas a sample definition that restricts attention to plausible DACA-eligible immigrants is likely  defensible. Similarly, a specification that controls for post-treatment variables is generally less defensible than one that conditions only on pretreatment covariates. Let $\R(P)$ denote the set of defensible research choices: combinations that are internally coherent, feasible in the data, and defensible ex ante given the stated question and standard econometric practice.

Let $\Ps(P)$ denote the underlying population distribution of observables, rich enough to construct any defensible sample restriction in $\mathcal{U}$ and any defensible outcome or treatment variable in $\mathcal{Y}$ and $\mathcal{T}$.

\begin{definition}
A defensible set of research choices is an element
\[
r = (u_r, y_r, t_r, s_r) \in \R(P) \subseteq \mathcal{U} \times \mathcal{Y} \times \mathcal{T} \times \mathcal{S}.
\]
Given a realized dataset $\omega$, implementing research choices $r$ produces an estimate $\hat\theta(r, \omega)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
When research problem $P$ is sufficiently specific to determine a single scientific object of interest, its target estimand is a scalar $\theta^\star(P)$.
\end{definition}
If the research problem is itself too ambiguous to pin down a unique benchmark object, one can instead treat $P$ as defining a set of defensible target estimands; throughout, we write $\theta^\star(P)$ for the benchmark scientific target the research problem is intended to describe.

\begin{definition}
Each defensible set of research choices $r \in \R(P)$ induces an implied estimand $\theta(r;P)$, corresponding to the causal or statistical object actually targeted by that choice of population, outcome, treatment, and specification.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
The \emph{estimand mismatch} of research choices $r$ relative to the target estimand is
\[
\delta(r;P) \equiv \theta(r;P) - \theta^\star(P).
\]
When $\delta(r;P)\neq 0$, dispersion across executed research choices mixes differences in estimation strategies with differences in targeted causal parameters.
\end{definition}

The broader causal-inference and statistics literatures distinguish the target estimand from the implied estimand and discuss mismatch between the intended question and the performed analysis \citep{blair2019declaring, choi2023question, chang2024alignment, barnard2026mismatch}. In our setting, mismatch arises when one specification defines full-time work as at least 35 hours per week while another uses 30 hours, annual weeks worked; similarly, alternative DACA eligibility proxies using different age and arrival cutoffs target different treated populations. Such choices differ not only in estimation strategy but in the object being estimated.

\subsection{LLMs as replication agents}
\label{sec:framework_llm}

Fix a research problem $P$ and a temperature parameter $\tau>0$, the standard sampling parameter governing how concentrated the model's next-token probabilities are. Higher $\tau$ increases the probability of lower-ranked continuations and therefore generates more variation across runs. In our setting, $\tau>0$ allows one fixed prompt to produce multiple non-identical sets of research choices.

Repeated independent runs of the same LLM on the same problem description induce a distribution over defensible research choices, $\pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$, supported on $\R(P)$. If $R \sim \pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$ and the dataset is fixed at $\omega$, the empirical object in this paper is the fixed-sample distribution of estimates $\hat\theta(R,\omega)$.

Why might LLMs produce plausible research choices? Their training corpora include academic articles, codebases, and empirical templates \citep{gao2020pile, chen2021codex}---the same materials from which researchers learn to define samples, write regression equations, and choose standard-error adjustments. LLM outputs can therefore be interpreted as draws from a compressed, noisy representation of standard empirical social science practice. We seek to test whether $\pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$ coincides with the distribution human researchers would generate.

\section{Computing Pipeline}
\label{sec:software}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This section describes how we generate repeated draws $\hat\theta(R,\omega)$ from $\pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$. Each run receives the same natural-language research prompt and the same dataset; only the AI's choices vary across runs. The pipeline records the full sequence from prompt to final numeric output, so each estimate can be traced back to the underlying specification and compared with the distribution generated by human analysts.

\paragraph{Phase~1: research choices.}
\label{sec:phase1}
The AI model receives a fixed description of the research question together with a data codebook. It returns a structured specification encoding four researcher choices: sample restrictions, outcome definition, treatment definition, and an estimator command that embeds controls, fixed effects, weighting, and variance-estimation decisions. A machine-readable contract validates each specification before any code runs. Because the LLM is called with positive randomness (temperature $\tau>0$), each call can yield a different set of choices.

\paragraph{Phase~2: execution.}
\label{sec:phase2}
The AI agent continues to write the analysis script inside an isolated workspace containing only the raw data and documentation files. Combining specification generation and execution in one session makes the workflow more reliable in practice: the model can immediately revise non-implementable choices after encountering null samples, missing variables, or runtime errors. The pipeline checks that each completed run produces a point estimate, standard error, and sample size.

\paragraph{Phase~3: aggregation.}
\label{sec:phase3}
The final phase aggregates the full archived run directory structure, records whether each session preserves a recoverable specification, classifies each usable specification's methodological features (model type, controls, weighting, standard-error adjustment) by parsing the estimator code, and produces the summary statistics, distributional plots, specification curves, and cross-tabulations reported in Section~\ref{sec:results}. The paper's quantitative analysis keeps only runs with recoverable specifications, numeric results, and positive standard errors.

\paragraph{Accessing AI Models.}
Our implementation of the computing pipeline relies on GitHub Copilot which grants access to a range of AI models. These include GPT models from OpenAI, Claude models from Anthropic, and Gemini models from Google. While our empirical application exlusively uses GPT models, our pipeline in principle could leverage any of these models or combination thereof. We leverage the Github Copilot CLI, a command-line interface facilitate programmatic calls to Copilot models, facilitating the automation and scaling of research-choice generation and execution.\footnote{An alternative is to use API calls to the same models. Direct API calls to model providers, however, are much more expensive (as of this writing) than leveraging personal or institutional subscriptions, such as through Github Education, Claude Code, or Codex. We did experiment with generating phase 1 choices from the Mistral API for their Devstral model, with the upside being the rare ability to control model temperature and random seed. Unfortunately, the Devstral model is not sufficiently good at tool-calling and therefore not reliable for phase 2. Many of its phase 1 specifications were therefore not executable.}

\section{Empirical Application}
\label{sec:results}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Our empirical object is the distribution of $\hat\theta(R,\omega)$ induced by $\pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$ for a fixed raw dataset~$\omega$. This dispersion reflects variation in research choices, rather than sampling uncertainty. We apply our approach to the question answered by many analysts in \citet{huntingtonklein2025sources}: how the immigration policy, Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals, DACA, affected full-time work among affected immigrants.

\subsection{The benchmark multi-analyst study}
\label{sec:benchmark}

\citet{huntingtonklein2025sources} recruit a large team of economists and graduate students and ask each to independently answer the same causal question using the same instructions and raw data: how did eligibility for DACA affect the probability of full-time employment among eligible immigrants? Participants are given a data codebook and the research question but otherwise exercise broad discretion over sample construction, variable definitions, and specification choices.

The study organizes submissions into three tasks that vary the degree of researcher discretion. \emph{Task~1}, the high-discretion condition, most closely resembles standard applied economics practice: analysts receive only the research question and codebook and make all methodological choices independently. \emph{Tasks~2} and \emph{3} impose progressively more structure, narrowing the choice set by prescribing sample restrictions and then estimation specification. Our pipeline mimics the Task~1 environment because our prompt fixes only the research question and data documentation, leaving all remaining choices to the AI agent.

Across Task~1 submissions, the point-estimate distribution in \citet{huntingtonklein2025sources} is wide: the unweighted mean is 0.053, the median is 0.030, and the IQR spans 0.014 to 0.051, with a maximum of 0.660. Sample sizes range considerably (median 179{,}960; IQR 61{,}600--356{,}787), reflecting a large divergence in how analysts bound their estimation samples even when given identical raw data. The human-analyst results therefore exhibit exactly the kind of researcher-induced heterogeneity this paper seeks to characterize and replicate computationally at scale.

Importantly for our purposes, \citet{huntingtonklein2025sources} have publicized the instructions and codebook used for the exercise. Their instructions direct authors to download whichever variables they deem necessary from specific ACS extracts on IPUMS. To streamline the LLM workflow and to curb the pressure on the IPUMS API, we instead make available to the AI agents a large extract with a wide range of potentially relevant variables. We also obtain a set of state policy variables and an associated codebook from the \citet{huntingtonklein2025sources} public repository and make it available to all AI agent runs.

\subsection{Summary statistics and distributions}
The final analytic sample contains 139 AI agent executions with recoverable specifications, numeric results, and positive standard errors.

The raw GitHub Copilot CLI archive contains 177 phase~1--2 session directories. Twenty-nine never preserve a recoverable JSON specification, so they cannot enter a specification-level analysis: 15 leave the placeholder \texttt{analysis.py} unchanged, 11 stop after writing \texttt{analysis.py} but before saving either a specification or results, and 3 end in CLI or authentication errors. The remaining 148 sessions do preserve a machine-readable specification, of which 125 come from GPT 5.1 Codex Mini and 23 from GPT 5.4.

Among those 148 spec-bearing runs, five remain unusable after execution: three collapse to samples with no treatment variation, one leaves no filtered observations, and one never returns the required combined JSON payload after repeated execution hangs. Two additional archived runs stopped before writing \texttt{results.json} even though the saved analysis scripts run successfully; rerunning those scripts recovers valid outputs. This leaves 143 numeric estimates. Four of those are degenerate zero-effect, zero-standard-error outputs, so the final analytic sample used below contains $N=139$ runs with recoverable specifications and positive standard errors.

Table~\ref{tab:nhk_summary} reports summary statistics for point estimates, standard errors, and sample sizes across AI agent executions. The pooled point-estimate distribution centers near zero and is wide, with a median of 0.004, IQR from $-0.042$ to 0.046, mean 0.006, and inverse-SE weighted mean $-0.006$. The full range spans $-0.396$ to 0.332. Sample sizes vary substantially (mean 105{,}957; median 82{,}264; IQR 53{,}232 to 125{,}521). Choices about year windows, age bounds, citizenship definitions, and immigration-timing rules continue to move the effective estimation sample considerably.

\subsection{Comparison to the benchmark multi-analyst study}

\citet{huntingtonklein2025sources} study the same causal question and report dispersion across human researchers. We design our pipeline outputs as like-for-like counterparts: effect-size distributions (Figures~\ref{fig:nhk_effects} and~\ref{fig:nhk_speccurve}), sample-size distributions (Figure~\ref{fig:sample_sizes}), and summary statistics (Table~\ref{tab:nhk_summary}).

The most directly comparable benchmark is Huntington-Klein et al.'s ``Task~1,'' the high-discretion condition in which analysts answer the same DACA--employment question while retaining broad freedom over sample construction, variable definitions, and specification choices. That is the closest analogue to our pipeline, which also leaves those choices to the AI agent. In Table~3 of \cite{huntingtonklein2025sources}, the unweighted Task~1 distribution has mean~0.053, median~0.030, and IQR from 0.014 to 0.051 (max~0.660). Our pooled GitHub Copilot CLI distribution has mean 0.006, median 0.004, and IQR from $-0.042$ to 0.046 (max~0.332). Sample-size dispersion remains substantial in both settings: the benchmark median is 179{,}960 (IQR 61{,}600--356{,}787) versus 82{,}264 (IQR 53{,}232--125{,}521) in our retained CLI sample.

Two caveats apply. Our distribution reflects (i)~the LLM-induced support over research choices under our prompt design and command-line execution environment and (ii)~selection into saved specifications and successful execution. Relative to the earlier mixed-provider draft, the CLI-only sample is cleaner but narrower: interface heterogeneity is held fixed, so the remaining dispersion is more clearly attributable to within-model research choices. The benchmark reports Task~1--3 objects under different constraints; our pipeline mirrors the high-discretion Task~1 setting.

Tables~\ref{tab:method_shares} and~\ref{tab:control_forms} report method shares and mean effects by control-variable functional form. In the benchmark, 82\% of submissions use linear regression, 22\% use no SE adjustment, and 25\% use sample weights. Our pooled Copilot CLI sample places even more mass on weighted linear models: 93.5\% of retained runs use OLS or WLS, 74.1\% use the ACS person weight, 38.1\% request robust HC standard errors, and 14.4\% request state-clustered standard errors. Mean effects also move with control structure: specifications including year fixed effects average 0.012 versus 0.000 without them, and specifications including state fixed effects average 0.022 versus $-0.003$ without them. Linear age controls are associated with more positive estimates (0.038) than specifications omitting age entirely ($-0.006$), although age controls still appear in a minority of retained runs. This is directionally consistent with the broader finding of \cite{huntingtonklein2025sources} that routine modeling choices materially affect estimates.

We view these comparisons as validation targets. Agreement in broad qualitative patterns supports stochastic replication agents as a scalable approximation to multi-analyst exercises; discrepancies reveal differences in what kinds of specifications each approach generates and can execute.

\subsection{Method mix within the Copilot CLI backbone}

The cleaned Copilot-only sample removes cross-provider heterogeneity from the paper's main quantitative results, but substantial variation remains within the retained specifications themselves. Table~\ref{tab:method_shares} shows that the workflow is concentrated in weighted linear models: 76.3\% of retained runs use WLS, 17.3\% use OLS, and only 6.5\% fall into a residual ``Other'' category consisting of logit or lightweight custom estimators. Relative to the benchmark human sample, GitHub Copilot CLI therefore appears more likely to settle on weighted linear regressions and, after adding the GPT 5.4 tranche, more likely to request clustered standard errors than the earlier GPT 5.1-only draft suggested.

Table~\ref{tab:control_forms} shows that even within this narrower design space, routine specification choices continue to move estimates. Year fixed effects shift the mean from 0.000 to 0.012, state fixed effects shift it from $-0.003$ to 0.022, and linear age controls shift it from $-0.006$ to 0.038. The magnitudes are smaller than the full spread of the distribution, but they reinforce the core point of the paper: once the interface is held fixed, familiar choices about controls, weights, and variance estimation still produce meaningful estimate heterogeneity.

\subsection{Differences between GPT 5.1 Codex Mini and GPT 5.4}

Table~\ref{tab:provider_comparison} compares the two Copilot model backbones within the same command-line workflow. The GPT 5.1 Codex Mini sample remains centered near zero: across 116 retained runs, the mean estimate is $-0.002$, the median is $-0.003$, and the IQR is $[-0.045,\,0.030]$. The 23 retained GPT 5.4 runs are notably more positive: the mean is 0.048, the median is 0.052, and even the 25th percentile remains positive at 0.031. GPT 5.4 is also more concentrated in methodological terms. Every retained GPT 5.4 run uses WLS and the ACS person weight, roughly 70\% request state-clustered standard errors, 83\% include year fixed effects, and 74\% include state fixed effects. By contrast, GPT 5.1 Codex Mini explores a broader design space with much less clustering and many more unweighted or non-fixed-effects specifications.

These model differences line up with differences in the executed samples. The median retained GPT 5.4 sample contains 37{,}930 observations versus 91{,}173 for GPT 5.1 Codex Mini, and the median standard error is correspondingly larger (0.0127 versus 0.0039). That helps explain why adding GPT 5.4 shifts the unweighted pooled distribution upward while leaving the inverse-SE weighted mean near $-0.006$. Substantively, the GPT 5.4 tranche appears to favor a more benchmark-like empirical template---weighted regressions with fixed effects and clustered standard errors---but on smaller, tighter samples that still produce noticeably positive point estimates. The within-CLI comparison therefore reinforces a broader lesson of the paper: even after fixing the interface and prompt, the particular frontier model used to instantiate the replication agent materially affects the induced distribution of estimates.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Standard errors measure sampling uncertainty but ignore the often-substantial variation that arises from defensible differences in how researchers construct samples, define variables, and specify models. This paper treats that variation as an estimable object. By fixing the implementation path to GitHub Copilot CLI and repeating the same research prompt across fresh sessions, we obtain a cleaner empirical distribution of researcher-induced uncertainty. In the DACA--employment application, the resulting 139 retained estimates have a pooled mean of 0.006, median 0.004, and IQR $[-0.042,\,0.046]$, with broad qualitative resemblance to the benchmark human-analyst study but clear sensitivity to the model used within the same CLI harness.

Our study has several limitations. First, the benchmark multi-analyst study, \cite{huntingtonklein2025sources}, may well have been included in the training data for the models we use: public project materials were available on OSF well before our runs, including the Many Economists public project from October 2022, the public \texttt{repl\_data} repository with DACA prepared-data documentation and code from August 2023, and the public 2025 discussion paper itself \citep{nickchk2022manyeconomists, nickchk2023repldata, huntingtonklein2025sources}. We therefore interpret the exercise as measuring how current LLMs recombine public empirical templates, not as a clean test of zero prior exposure. Second, different defensible specifications can imply different estimands by changing the treated population, timing window, or treatment definition. Third, although the cleaner GitHub Copilot CLI design improves comparability across runs by holding the interface fixed, the GPT 5.1 Codex Mini and GPT 5.4 tranches still differ materially in method mix, sample construction, and the location of the resulting estimate distribution. Model choice therefore remains an important source of researcher-induced uncertainty even within one command-line harness.

For empirical practice, the framework offers a diagnostic: researchers can run the pipeline on their own question and data to assess how sensitive results are to the space of defensible alternatives, flagging specifications that produce qualitatively different conclusions. The open-source pipeline makes this diagnostic reproducible.

Extending the approach in two directions would strengthen it most. First, calibrating the LLM-induced distribution to human analyst distributions---perhaps by fine-tuning on existing multi-analyst datasets---would sharpen the proxy's usefulness. Second, combining researcher-choice resampling with data resampling (e.g., bootstrap) would yield joint uncertainty measures that account for both sources of variation simultaneously.

Three design principles guide the architecture: (i)~\emph{auditability}---every run stores a full provenance chain from the research-problem description through the final numeric output; (ii)~\emph{isolation}---each execution runs in its own directory with no shared state, so that research choices are independent draws; and (iii)~\emph{portability}---resource locations resolve dynamically, allowing the pipeline to run across operating systems and local environments without code changes.

\subsection*{Specification contract}

Phase~1 requires the LLM to return a JSON object with four fields:
\begin{Verbatim}[fontsize=\small, frame=single]
{
  "sample_selection":        [<filter condition>, ...],
  "outcome_definition":      "<Python expression>",
  "treatment_definition":    "<Python expression>",
  "model_specification_line": "<executable Python line>"
}
\end{Verbatim}
The contract is deliberately minimal: it pins down what every set of research choices must produce (an estimator applied to an outcome, treatment, and sample) while leaving maximum freedom in how those objects are defined. The \texttt{model\_specification\_line} must be a single executable Python statement calling an estimator (e.g., \texttt{smf.ols(...).fit()}) and may embed controls, fixed effects, sample weights, and clustering choices. The contract is enforced by a JSON Schema validated before any code executes.

For the paper-ready implementation, Phase~1 is executed through GitHub Copilot CLI. The system extracts the JSON object from the model response, validates it against the schema, and stores metadata for auditing.

\subsection*{Execution sandbox}

Phase~2 takes each validated specification and produces an executable analysis script plus numeric results. The steps are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Create an isolated workspace.} Each run receives only whitelisted input data and documentation files.
\item \textbf{Link large inputs.} Large data assets are shared across runs via platform-appropriate linking, with safe fallbacks.
\item \textbf{Invoke a coding agent.} The agent receives the structured specification, generates analysis code, and executes it with bounded self-correction for runtime errors.
\item \textbf{Validate output.} The pipeline verifies the result object exists, parses correctly, and contains numeric fields for point estimate, standard error, and sample size. Runs that fail validation, never write a results file, or produce degenerate outputs with non-positive standard errors are flagged explicitly in the archive.
\end{enumerate}
A combined mode allows a single agent session to both propose and implement a specification, useful for agents that benefit from end-to-end context.

\subsection*{Aggregation and classification}

Phase~3 aggregates the full session archive, not just the subset that already has clean final outputs. Each archived session receives a specification-status label (recoverable JSON specification versus missing specification) and an execution-status label (successful result, validation failure, interrupted run with no results file, or degenerate numeric output with non-positive standard error). For sessions with recoverable specifications, the aggregator then infers derived metadata from \texttt{model\_specification\_line} using regular-expression classifiers:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Model type}: OLS, WLS, Logit, or Other, from the estimator function name.
\item \textbf{Controls and fixed effects}: parsed from the formula's right-hand side, distinguishing continuous regressors from categorical (\texttt{C(...)}) terms.
\item \textbf{Sample weighting}: detected from \texttt{weights=} arguments (typically the ACS person weight \texttt{PERWT}).
\item \textbf{SE adjustment}: classified as clustered (state), heteroskedasticity-robust (HC), or none, from \texttt{cov\_type} and \texttt{cov\_kwds} arguments.
\end{itemize}
This classification operates on structured text (Python code), avoiding reliance on self-reported LLM metadata. The paper's quantitative analysis then keeps only sessions with recoverable specifications and \texttt{execution\_status = success}.

Phase~4 produces:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Summary statistics} (Table~\ref{tab:nhk_summary}): Unweighted and inverse-SE-weighted moments and quantiles of the point-estimate distribution, plus standard-error and sample-size distributions.
\item \textbf{Density plots} (Figures~\ref{fig:nhk_effects}--\ref{fig:sample_sizes}): Epanechnikov kernel densities with box-and-whisker overlays.
\item \textbf{Specification curve} (Figure~\ref{fig:nhk_speccurve}): Estimates ordered by magnitude with 95\% confidence intervals.
\item \textbf{Decision-share tables} (Tables~\ref{tab:method_shares}--\ref{tab:control_forms}): Cross-tabulations of estimation methods, weighting, SE adjustments, and control-variable functional forms.
\end{itemize}
All tables and figures are generated from the same integrated analysis dataset so that manuscript objects update automatically when the pipeline is re-run.

\section{Prompt excerpt and design}
\label{app:prompt}

This appendix reproduces the key portion of the prompt used to elicit structured specifications of research choices. The original prompt also included extensive variable definitions and codebook material to support independent implementation; we omit that bulk for space and instead present the instructions that govern the required outputs and the feasible choice set.

\noindent\textit{Prompt text (governing instructions and required output).}
\begin{Verbatim}[fontsize=\small]
Research question:
Among ethnically Hispanic-Mexican Mexican-born people living in the United States,
what was the causal impact of eligibility for the Deferred Action for Childhood
Arrivals (DACA) program on the probability that the eligible person is employed
full-time, defined as usually working 35 hours per week or more?

DACA was implemented in 2012. Estimate effects on full-time employment in 2013–2016.

KEY DELIVERABLES — ONLY RETURN THE FOLLOWING USING PYTHON:
Your answer MUST contain ONLY a JSON block with the following structure:

{
  "sample_selection": ["<filter condition 1>", "<filter condition 2>", "..."],
  "outcome_definition": "<Python expression for outcome variable>",
  "treatment_definition": "<Python expression for DACA eligibility>",
  "model_specification_line": "<exact Python line calling the estimator>"
}

Rules:
- Provide implementable filters and expressions consistent with the available data.
- The model_specification_line must be a single executable Python line that:
  (i) estimates the effect of treatment_definition on outcome_definition; and
  (ii) encodes any controls, fixed effects, weights, and SE/clustering choices.
- Be explicit in sample_selection about years, age bounds, nativity/origin criteria,
  and any restrictions used to approximate eligibility rules.
- Do NOT include any commentary outside the JSON block.
\end{Verbatim}

The prompt fixes the research question and the required output contract but allows discretion over sample restrictions, functional form, controls, fixed effects, weighting, and variance estimation. This discretion is the mechanism that generates dispersion across executed estimates when the prompt is held fixed and the generator is stochastic.
