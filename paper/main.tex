\begin{abstract}
Empirical papers report standard errors that quantify sampling uncertainty conditional on a particular set of researcher decisions, but reasonable alternatives in samples, variable definitions, and specifications can yield different estimates from the same underlying data. We define an econometric object for this additional uncertainty: the distribution of estimates induced by admissible researcher workflows, and we show how large language models (LLMs) can serve as stochastic replication agents to approximate it at scale. With identical prompts and positive temperature, independent runs generate distinct workflows; our framework separates workflow-induced variation from sampling variation and highlights \emph{estimand drift} when workflows change the implicit target population or treatment definition. We apply the approach to the DACA full-time employment question studied by \citet{huntingtonklein2025sources}, implementing a pipeline that executes each workflow in isolated run environments and summarizes the induced distribution of estimates.

\medskip
\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} Researcher degrees of freedom, replication, specification uncertainty, multi-analyst studies, Large Language Models

\medskip
\noindent \textbf{JEL Codes:} C12, C15, C18, C52
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Empirical papers routinely report standard errors that quantify sampling uncertainty. These standard errors are conditional on a set of researcher decisions: which dataset to analyze, how to define the estimation sample, how to construct outcomes and treatments, and which specification and standard-error adjustments to use. A growing body of evidence shows that reasonable researchers making different (and often defensible) decisions can produce meaningfully different estimates from the same underlying data \citep{silberzahn2018many, huntington2021influence, breznau2022observing}. This implies an additional source of uncertainty beyond sampling variation: \emph{workflow-induced} (researcher-induced) variation.

The difficulty is measurement. Multi-analyst studies are expensive, requiring recruitment and coordination. Pre-analysis plans constrain discretion but do not measure the sensitivity of results to alternative, still-defensible workflows \citep{olken2015promises}. Specification-curve and multiverse analyses require the original analyst to enumerate and implement alternatives \citep{simonsohn2020specification, steegen2016multiverse}.

We make three contributions. First, we formalize an econometric target for researcher-induced uncertainty by treating a \emph{researcher workflow} as the full mapping from a fixed prompt and dataset to an executed estimator, and by defining the resulting distribution of executed estimates induced by a distribution over admissible workflows. Second, we show how large language models (LLMs) can be used as stochastic replication agents to approximate this distribution at scale: with an identical prompt and positive temperature, repeated independent runs generate different, interpretable workflows that can be executed end-to-end on the same data. Third, we implement an auditable pipeline that makes the full chain of choices---specification, implementation, execution, and aggregation---transparent and reproducible.

We emphasize two distinctions that are essential for interpretation.
First, the distribution across executed workflows is different from the sampling distribution: if all runs use the same dataset, variation reflects workflow-induced heterogeneity (and its interaction with the realized sample), not classical sampling error.
Second, workflow variation can include \emph{estimand drift}: two workflows may implicitly target different causal parameters by changing the treated population, the timing window, or the treatment definition. We therefore treat workflow-induced variation as an object of interest in its own right and report diagnostics and stratifications where possible.

We apply the approach to a replication-at-scale exercise calibrated to the benchmark multi-analyst study by \citet{huntingtonklein2025sources}, which asks how DACA eligibility affects the probability of working full-time. We generate structured specifications from an LLM, execute each workflow in an isolated run directory, aggregate outputs into a standardized dataset, and produce publication-ready tables and figures designed to match the benchmark study’s descriptive objects. Section \ref{sec:framework} defines the object and its interpretation; Section \ref{sec:nhk_pipeline} documents the replication-at-scale pipeline; Section \ref{sec:results} reports results and comparisons; and Section \ref{sec:limits} discusses limitations and external validity.

\paragraph{Related literature.}
This paper builds on work on researcher degrees of freedom and multiverse approaches that emphasize sensitivity to defensible analytic choices \citep{simmons2011false, gelman2013garden, steegen2016multiverse, simonsohn2020specification}. Relative to analyst-enumerated multiverses, our emphasis is on an estimand and an empirical object: a distribution of executed estimates induced by an explicit distribution over workflows, where a workflow bundles sample selection, variable construction, specification, and variance estimation.

We also relate to multi-analyst studies that measure researcher-induced variation directly by recruiting analysts to study the same question on the same data \citep{silberzahn2018many, huntington2021influence, breznau2022observing}. We use these exercises as validation benchmarks for replication at scale: if a stochastic workflow generator produces broadly similar dispersion patterns and decision shares, it can provide a scalable complement to human-intensive designs, while disagreements are informative about differences in workflow support and implementation constraints.

Finally, our approach is connected to resampling and Monte Carlo ideas, where repeated draws are used to approximate the distribution of a statistic \citep{efron1979bootstrap, andrews2000bootstrap}. Repeated LLM runs play a similar computational role for the induced workflow distribution (not for sampling uncertainty unless one also resamples data). We also connect to emerging work on large language models as economic agents and research tools \citep{horton2023large, brand2023gpt, korinek2023generative}; here, the LLM is neither a black-box predictor nor a writing assistant, but a stochastic generator of executable research workflows.

\section{Framework: workflow-induced uncertainty}
\label{sec:framework}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Estimand and data}

Fix a prompt $P$ that states the research question and (to the extent possible) pins down the target population, outcome, treatment, and timing. Let $\Ps(P)$ denote the corresponding population distribution over observables and potential outcomes. We use potential-outcomes notation to keep the estimand well-defined when the treatment is binary.

\begin{definition}[Prompt-level target estimand]
Let $D \in \{0,1\}$ be a treatment (e.g., DACA eligibility). Let $Y(1)$ and $Y(0)$ denote potential outcomes. The prompt-level target estimand is a scalar $\theta^\star(P)$. A canonical example is the average treatment effect (ATE)
\[
\theta^\star(P) \equiv \E_{\Ps(P)}[Y(1) - Y(0)].
\]
In applications, $P$ may define a subpopulation (e.g., foreign-born Hispanic noncitizens) by conditioning on covariates; in that case $\Ps(P)$ and $\theta^\star(P)$ are understood to be restricted accordingly.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Workflow-level estimand and estimand drift]
A workflow can change the effective target population or treatment/outcome definition through its sample restrictions and variable construction. We therefore associate each workflow $r \in \R$ with a (possibly implicit) workflow-level estimand $\theta(r;P)$ and define its estimand drift relative to the prompt-level target as
\[
\delta(r;P) \equiv \theta(r;P) - \theta^\star(P).
\]
When $\delta(r;P)\neq 0$, dispersion across executed workflows mixes differences in estimation strategies with differences in targeted causal parameters.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Workflows}

We define a workflow as the full set of choices required to map data and a research question into an executed estimator.

\begin{definition}[Researcher workflow]
A workflow is an element $r \in \R$ that bundles:
\begin{itemize}
\item sample selection rules,
\item variable definitions (outcome, treatment, covariates),
\item a model specification (functional form, controls, fixed effects), and
\item an estimation and variance procedure.
\end{itemize}
Given a realized dataset $\omega$ and workflow $r$, executing the workflow produces an estimate $\hat\theta(r, \omega)$, which we interpret as an estimator for $\theta(r;P)$ when execution is successful.
\end{definition}

\subsection{A decomposition and the role of estimand drift}
Let $R$ denote a random workflow drawn from a distribution $\pi$ over $\R$. Let $\Omega$ denote a random sample (dataset) drawn from $\Ps(P)$. The total dispersion of executed estimates across workflows and samples can be decomposed.

\begin{proposition}[Workflow/sampling variance decomposition]
\label{prop:decomp}
Assume $\E[\hat\theta(R,\Omega)^2] < \infty$. Then
\[
\Var\big(\hat\theta(R,\Omega)\big)
= \E\big[\Var(\hat\theta(R,\Omega)\mid R)\big]
 + \Var\big(\E[\hat\theta(R,\Omega)\mid R]\big).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}[Interpretation]
The first term is sampling variability averaged across workflows; the second term is workflow-induced heterogeneity in the workflow-specific expected estimate. In a fixed-sample setting (as in our DACA application), variation across LLM runs describes the dispersion of $\hat\theta(R,\omega)$ conditional on the fixed realized dataset $\omega$.
\end{remark}

The decomposition in Proposition \ref{prop:decomp} does not by itself guarantee that workflow-induced heterogeneity reflects ``researcher variance holding the estimand fixed.'' A convenient bookkeeping identity is
\[
\hat\theta(r,\omega) - \theta^\star(P)
=
\underbrace{\big(\hat\theta(r,\omega) - \theta(r;P)\big)}_{\text{estimation error}}
+
\underbrace{\delta(r;P)}_{\text{estimand drift}}.
\]
If workflows change the target population or the treatment definition, then $\delta(r;P)$ may be nonzero, and dispersion across executed workflows reflects both differences in estimation strategies and differences in targeted causal parameters. In our application, we therefore report (i) the distribution of executed estimates and (ii) diagnostics based on structured specification fields (sample selection, outcome and treatment definitions) to assess comparability.

\subsection{Low marginal cost, selective reporting, and the number of runs}
Replication at scale is motivated by a simple concern: when the marginal cost of trying another reasonable workflow is low, an analyst can try many workflows and (absent full disclosure or adjustment) selectively report the most favorable one. This is the familiar intuition behind researcher degrees of freedom and ``p-hacking'' \citep{simmons2011false, gelman2013garden}.

To formalize the comparative static as simply as possible, suppose an analyst runs $K$ workflows and obtains p-values $p_1,\dots,p_K$ for a fixed null hypothesis. Under the null, if $p_j\stackrel{i.i.d.}{\sim}\mathrm{Unif}(0,1)$ and the analyst reports the minimum p-value $p_{(1)}\equiv \min_j p_j$, then the probability of reporting at least one ``significant'' result increases mechanically with $K$.

\begin{proposition}[Selective reporting increases with the number of attempts]
\label{prop:selective_reporting}
Let $p_1,\dots,p_K$ be i.i.d. $\mathrm{Unif}(0,1)$ and define $p_{(1)}\equiv \min_{1\le j\le K} p_j$. Then for any $\alpha\in(0,1)$,
\[
\Pr\big(p_{(1)}\le \alpha\big)=1-(1-\alpha)^K,
\]
which is increasing in $K$.
\end{proposition}

The i.i.d.\ assumption is a simplification---workflows are typically dependent---but the basic point is robust: selecting an extreme among more attempts distorts reported evidence absent disclosure or adjustment. Our approach does not eliminate the incentive to cherry-pick; instead it changes the default reporting object. We commit ex ante to a prompt $P$, generate workflows under an explicit stochastic mechanism, and report the induced distribution of executed estimates rather than a single selected specification.

The number of runs $K$ also governs Monte Carlo precision for estimated features of the induced distribution (means, quantiles, tail probabilities). Under independent runs and finite variance, Monte Carlo standard errors shrink at the usual $K^{-1/2}$ rate. Practical guidance on choosing the number of repetitions for simulation-based estimators (e.g., \citealp{andrews2000bootstrap, andrews2001evaluation}) provides a natural starting point for choosing $K$ in replication-at-scale exercises.

\subsection{LLMs as a workflow generator}

Fix a prompt $P$ that states the research question and defines required objects. For a given LLM and temperature parameter $\tau>0$, repeated runs of the model with identical $P$ generate a distribution over workflows, which we denote $\pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$. Our empirical object is the distribution of executed estimates induced by $\pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}$ in a fixed dataset setting.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Replication at scale with stochastic replication agents}
\label{alg:pipeline}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Prompt $P$; number of runs $K$; LLM with temperature $\tau>0$; fixed dataset $\omega$
\For{$k=1,\dots,K$}
\State Sample a workflow $r_k \sim \pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$
\State Implement and execute $r_k$ on $\omega$ in an isolated run directory
\State Record $(\hat\theta_k, \widehat{SE}_k, n_k)$
\EndFor
\State Summarize the distribution of $\{\hat\theta_k\}_{k=1}^K$ and relate it to workflow features
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Replication-at-scale design: DACA replication pipeline}
\label{sec:nhk_pipeline}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We implement Algorithm \ref{alg:pipeline} in a four-phase pipeline whose artifacts are designed to be auditable and reproducible.

\noindent\textit{Phase 1: specification generation.} The LLM receives a standardized prompt and returns a structured JSON specification describing sample selection, outcome construction, treatment definition, and an executable model specification line.

\noindent\textit{Phase 2: implementation and execution.} For each specification, an agent implements an executable analysis script and runs it in an isolated per-run folder that contains only whitelisted inputs. Successful runs must produce a machine-validated JSON output containing a point estimate, standard error, and sample size.

\noindent\textit{Phase 3: aggregation.} We combine specifications and execution results into a single dataset, \texttt{runs\_complete.csv}, and infer additional metadata (e.g., fixed-effects indicators) from the model specification line.

\noindent\textit{Phase 4: meta-analysis.} We produce publication-ready tables and figures summarizing the resulting distribution of estimates and its relationship to workflow components.

\subsection{Summary statistics}

Table \ref{tab:nhk_summary} reports summary statistics for point estimates, standard errors, and sample sizes across successful executed workflows.

Across $N=38$ successful executions, the point-estimate distribution is centered near zero (median 0.003) but dispersed, with an interquartile range from $-0.008$ to 0.031 and pronounced tails. The summary table reports a mean of 0.019 (unweighted) and 0.021 (inverse-standard-error weighted), while the extrema reach -0.195 and 0.456. This combination---a near-zero center with fat tails---is consistent with the premise of multi-analyst work: even within a single dataset and research question, defensible workflows can imply meaningfully different estimates.

Sample sizes also vary widely across workflows (median 123{,}602; 25th percentile 61{,}288; 75th percentile 258{,}390), reflecting heterogeneity in sample restrictions and cohort definitions.

\subsection{Like-for-like comparison to the benchmark multi-analyst paper}

The benchmark multi-analyst study by \citet{huntingtonklein2025sources} analyzes the same underlying causal question and reports descriptive tables and figures summarizing dispersion across human researchers. Our Phase 4 outputs are designed to provide like-for-like descriptive counterparts: distributions of reported effect sizes (Figures \ref{fig:nhk_effects} and \ref{fig:nhk_speccurve}), the distribution of sample sizes (Figure \ref{fig:sample_sizes}), and summary statistics (Table \ref{tab:nhk_summary}).

The most directly comparable benchmark objects are those for ``Task 1,'' which grants substantial researcher freedom in specification and implementation. In the benchmark study’s Table 3 (Task 1), the unweighted distribution has mean 0.053, median 0.030, and an interquartile range from 0.014 to 0.051 (max 0.660). In our executed LLM workflow sample, the unweighted distribution has mean 0.019, median 0.003, and an interquartile range from -0.008 to 0.031 (max 0.456). Sample-size dispersion is also large in both settings: the benchmark Task 1 median sample size is 179{,}960 (25th percentile 61{,}600; 75th percentile 356{,}787), while our median is 123{,}602 (25th percentile 61{,}288; 75th percentile 258{,}390).

These comparisons should be interpreted cautiously. Our distribution is computed over successful executions in a particular implementation environment and therefore reflects (i) the LLM-induced workflow support under our prompt design and (ii) selection into what can be executed successfully. Moreover, the benchmark reports Task 1--3 objects under different constraints; our pipeline is designed to mirror the high-discretion Task 1 setting rather than the more constrained tasks.

Beyond these core descriptive objects, we also report method and specification diagnostics that mirror the benchmark’s emphasis on how analytic decisions map into results. Table \ref{tab:method_shares} reports shares of workflows by estimation method, weighting, and variance adjustment, and Table \ref{tab:control_forms} reports how the mean effect differs across common control-variable functional forms.

Benchmark Table 4 reports that 82\% of submissions use linear regression, 22\% use no standard-error adjustment, and 25\% use sample weights (with substantial heterogeneity in clustering choices). Our executed workflows put similar mass on linear models in the broad sense (84.2\% labeled OLS or WLS), but differ in how frequently they apply weights and clustering: 50.0\% use \texttt{PERWT} and 65.8\% request state-clustered standard errors. Benchmark Table 5 shows that mean effects vary with control functional forms (e.g., for age: mean 0.058 with linear age controls vs 0.024 with age fixed effects). In our runs, mean effects vary sharply across inclusion and functional form as well, especially for specifications that omit key controls (e.g., mean 0.052 when age is omitted).

For transparency, we have chosen objects that mirror the benchmark paper’s descriptive summaries: our Table \ref{tab:nhk_summary} corresponds to their Task 1 distribution table; our Tables \ref{tab:method_shares} and \ref{tab:control_forms} correspond to their decision-share and functional-form summaries; and Figures \ref{fig:sample_sizes}, \ref{fig:nhk_effects}, and \ref{fig:nhk_speccurve} correspond to their sample-size distribution, effect-size distribution, and specification curve. We view these comparisons as validation targets: agreement in broad qualitative patterns would support the use of stochastic replication agents as a scalable approximation to multi-analyst exercises, while discrepancies are informative about differences in workflow support (what kinds of specifications are generated) and execution constraints (what kinds of specifications can be implemented successfully).

\section{Limitations and external validity}
\label{sec:limits}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Our approach is descriptive and its interpretation depends on the relationship between the LLM-induced workflow distribution and the set of workflows a population of human researchers would generate.

LLMs reflect training-data priors and coding conventions. This affects which workflows are generated and how often, and it can place some reasonable research designs effectively outside the model’s support.

When all workflows run on the same realized dataset, the induced dispersion is not a sampling distribution. It describes heterogeneity across workflows conditional on the realized sample. In addition, our empirical distribution is computed over successful executions, and failures (or hard validation rules) may be correlated with workflow complexity or novelty, inducing selection.

Finally, workflows can alter the implicit estimand by changing the target population, timing window, or treatment definition. We treat this as part of the object of interest, but it complicates causal interpretation relative to a single fixed estimand.

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We propose a replication-at-scale framework that treats researcher workflows as a source of uncertainty and uses stochastic replication agents to approximate the induced distribution of executed estimates. The framework is explicit about interpretation: in a fixed-sample setting, dispersion reflects workflow-induced heterogeneity (and its interaction with the realized dataset), and workflow variation can include estimand drift when choices change the implicit target population or treatment definition. In our DACA application, the induced distribution is centered near zero but has wide dispersion and fat tails, and several descriptive patterns resemble those reported in the benchmark multi-analyst study.

At the same time, replication at scale has clear limitations. The induced distribution depends on the workflow support generated by the model and on selection into what can be executed successfully under a given set of implementation and validation rules. More broadly, using repeated runs to approximate a workflow distribution is conceptually distinct from estimating sampling uncertainty unless one also resamples data. Future work can strengthen the approach by calibrating the stochastic workflow generator to human analyst distributions, improving schema constraints and validation so that workflow variation reflects economic choices rather than implementation artifacts, and extending replication-at-scale designs to multiple datasets and research questions.

\section{Proof of Proposition \ref{prop:decomp}}
\label{app:proof_decomp}

Proposition \ref{prop:decomp} is the law of total variance. For any square-integrable random variable $X$ and conditioning variable $Z$,
\[
\Var(X) = \E[\Var(X\mid Z)] + \Var(\E[X\mid Z]).
\]
Apply this with $X=\hat\theta(R,\Omega)$ and $Z=R$.

\section{Proof of Proposition \ref{prop:selective_reporting}}
\label{app:proof_selective}

Under the assumptions of Proposition \ref{prop:selective_reporting},
\[
\Pr\big(p_{(1)} > \alpha\big)=\Pr\big(p_1>\alpha,\dots,p_K>\alpha\big)=(1-\alpha)^K.
\]
Taking complements yields $\Pr\big(p_{(1)}\le \alpha\big)=1-(1-\alpha)^K$, which is increasing in $K$ for any $\alpha\in(0,1)$.

\section{Prompt excerpt and design}
\label{app:prompt}

This appendix reproduces the key portion of the prompt used to elicit structured workflow specifications. The original prompt also included extensive variable definitions and codebook material to support independent implementation; we omit that bulk for space and instead present the instructions that govern the required outputs and the feasible choice set.

\noindent\textit{Prompt text (governing instructions and required output).}
\begin{Verbatim}[fontsize=\small]
Research question:
