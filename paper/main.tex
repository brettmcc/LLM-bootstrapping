\begin{abstract}
Reported standard errors capture sampling uncertainty conditional on one set of researcher decisions, but defensible alternatives in sample construction and specification can shift estimates substantially. We use repeated, independent LLM runs as stochastic replication agents---each receiving the same prompt and dataset but generating different research choices---to measure this researcher-induced variation. Applying the method to the immigration policy--employment question studied by \citet{huntingtonklein2025sources}, 86  LLM specifications yield estimates with an interquartile range of $[-0.023,\, 0.038]$, broadly matching the dispersion in \cite{huntingtonklein2025sources}. Our analysis supports using LLM agents as scalable proxies for multi-analyst designs.

\medskip
\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} Researcher degrees of freedom, replication, specification uncertainty, Large Language Models

\medskip
\noindent \textbf{JEL Codes:} C12, C15, C18
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Empirical papers routinely report standard errors that quantify sampling uncertainty. These standard errors are conditional on a set of researcher decisions: which dataset to analyze, how to define the estimation sample, how to construct outcomes and treatments, and which specification and standard-error adjustments to use. A growing body of evidence shows that researchers making different  decisions can produce meaningfully different estimates from the same underlying data \citep{silberzahn2018many, huntington2021influence, breznau2022observing}. This implies an additional source of uncertainty beyond standard errors capturing sampling variation: researcher degrees of freedom.

Measuring parameter uncertainty due to researcher degrees of freedom at scale is challenging. Multi-analyst studies are expensive, requiring extensive recruitment and coordination. Pre-analysis plans constrain discretion but do not measure the sensitivity of results to alternative, still-defensible research choices \citep{olken2015promises}. Specification-curve and multiverse analyses require the original analyst to enumerate and implement various alternatives \citep{simonsohn2020specification, steegen2016multiverse}.

We overcome these limitations using a novel approach: generative AI agents can perform empirical analyses at scale. We apply this approach to the DACA--employment question studied by many analysts in \citet{huntingtonklein2025sources}: how does eligibility for the Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals program affect the probability of full-time employment? Across 86  specifications generated by the pipeline, we obtain an interquartile range from $-0.023$ to 0.038. Sample sizes vary by roughly an order of magnitude (IQR 63{,}778 to 242{,}743). This combination---a near-zero center with fat tails and wide sample-size heterogeneity---broadly resembles the patterns from the benchmark multi-analyst study, in which human researchers produce an IQR from 0.014 to 0.051 under comparable high-discretion conditions.

Two distinctions are essential for interpretation. First, the distribution of executed estimates is not a sampling distribution: because all runs use the same realized dataset, dispersion reflects heterogeneity in research choices, not classical sampling error. Second, different research choices can create \emph{estimand mismatch}---two defensible specifications may implicitly target different causal parameters by changing the treated population, timing window, or treatment definition \citep{lundberg2021estimand}.

We make two main contributions. First, we provide an open-source, scalable approach towards multi-analyst designs, allowing researchers to quantify researcher-induced uncertainty. Second, we show that repeated LLM runs provide a computationally tractable way to approximate the distribution of human research choices at scale: each run generates an interpretable, machine-readable specification that can be executed without human intervention.

\paragraph{Related literature.}
Our work builds on the researcher degrees of freedom literature, which documents how defensible analytic choices affect results \citep{simmons2011false, gelman2013garden, steegen2016multiverse, simonsohn2020specification}. Relative to analyst-enumerated multiverses, we introduce AI-enumerated multiverses across the entire research pipeline, allowing for quantification of analyst-induced estimate uncertainty at scale.

Repeated LLM runs play a role analogous to bootstrap resampling \citep{efron1979bootstrap, andrews2000bootstrap}, approximating a distribution through repeated draws---here over research choices rather than data. We also connect to emerging work on LLMs as economic agents and research tools \citep{horton2023large, brand2023gpt, korinek2023generative, novymarx2026ai}.

Section~\ref{sec:framework} formalizes the research-choice space and the LLM-induced distribution. Section~\ref{sec:software} documents the three-phase pipeline architecture. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports the distribution of executed estimates, compares it to the human-analyst benchmark, and discusses heterogeneity across LLM models. The conclusion discusses the main limitations and implications.

\section{Framework: researcher-induced uncertainty}
\label{sec:framework}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We formalize the objects of interest in two steps. First, we define the research problem, the target estimand, and the set of defensible research choices. We then treat repeated LLM runs as a stochastic mechanism that samples from that set.

\subsection{Scientific target and defensible research choices}
\label{sec:framework_target}

A research question asks how treatment $T$ affects outcome $Y$ in a given population $U$. Define a research problem $P$ as a tuple of defensible choice sets,
\[
P = (\mathcal{U},\mathcal{Y},\mathcal{T},\mathcal{S}),
\]
where $\mathcal{U}$ is the set of defensible sample definitions, $\mathcal{Y}$ is the set of defensible outcome constructions, $\mathcal{T}$ is the set of defensible treatment constructions, and $\mathcal{S}$ is the set of defensible specifications. An element of $\mathcal{S}$ collects the control set, functional form, estimator, weighting, fixed effects, and variance-estimation choices such as clustering.

For example, in the DACA application researchers may make different defensible choices about age bounds, full-time work definitions, eligibility proxies, controls, and standard-error adjustments. Different combinations of these choices imply different research designs and, in some cases, different causal parameters.

Not every logically possible combination is defensible. A sample definition that includes only U.S. citizens would be inconsistent with the research question, whereas one that restricts attention to plausible DACA-eligible immigrants may be defensible. Similarly, a specification that controls for post-treatment variables is generally less defensible than one that conditions only on pretreatment covariates. Let $\R(P)$ denote the set of defensible research choices: combinations that are internally coherent, feasible in the data, and defensible ex ante given the stated question and standard econometric practice.

Let $\Ps(P)$ denote the underlying population distribution of observables, rich enough to construct any defensible sample restriction in $\mathcal{U}$ and any defensible outcome or treatment variable in $\mathcal{Y}$ and $\mathcal{T}$.

\begin{definition}
A defensible set of research choices is an element
\[
r = (u_r, y_r, t_r, s_r) \in \R(P) \subseteq \mathcal{U} \times \mathcal{Y} \times \mathcal{T} \times \mathcal{S}.
\]
Given a realized dataset $\omega$, implementing research choices $r$ produces an estimate $\hat\theta(r, \omega)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
When research problem $P$ is sufficiently specific to determine a single scientific object of interest, its target estimand is a scalar $\theta^\star(P)$.
\end{definition}
If the research problem is itself too ambiguous to pin down a unique benchmark object, one can instead treat $P$ as defining a set of defensible target estimands; throughout, we write $\theta^\star(P)$ for the benchmark scientific target the research problem is intended to describe.

\begin{definition}
Each defensible set of research choices $r \in \R(P)$ induces an implied estimand $\theta(r;P)$, corresponding to the causal or statistical object actually targeted by that choice of population, outcome, treatment, and specification.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
The \emph{estimand mismatch} of research choices $r$ relative to the target estimand is
\[
\delta(r;P) \equiv \theta(r;P) - \theta^\star(P).
\]
When $\delta(r;P)\neq 0$, dispersion across executed research choices mixes differences in estimation strategies with differences in targeted causal parameters.
\end{definition}

The broader causal-inference and statistics literatures distinguish the target estimand from the implied estimand and discuss mismatch between the intended question and the performed analysis \citep{blair2019declaring, choi2023question, chang2024alignment, barnard2026mismatch}. In our setting, mismatch arises when one specification defines full-time work as at least 35 hours per week while another uses 30 hours, annual weeks worked; similarly, alternative DACA eligibility proxies using different age and arrival cutoffs target different treated populations. Such choices differ not only in estimation strategy but in the object being estimated.

\subsection{LLMs as replication agents}
\label{sec:framework_llm}

Fix a research problem $P$ and a temperature parameter $\tau>0$, the standard sampling parameter governing how concentrated the model's next-token probabilities are. Higher $\tau$ increases the probability of lower-ranked continuations and therefore generates more variation across runs. In our setting, $\tau>0$ allows one fixed prompt to produce multiple non-identical sets of research choices.

Repeated independent runs of the same LLM on the same problem description induce a distribution over defensible research choices, $\pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$, supported on $\R(P)$. If $R \sim \pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$ and the dataset is fixed at $\omega$, the empirical object in this paper is the fixed-sample distribution of estimates $\hat\theta(R,\omega)$.

Why might LLMs produce plausible research choices? Their training corpora include academic articles, codebases, and empirical templates \citep{gao2020pile, chen2021codex}---the same materials from which researchers learn to define samples, write regression equations, and choose standard-error adjustments. LLM outputs can therefore be interpreted as draws from a compressed, noisy representation of standard empirical social science practice. We seek to test whether $\pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$ coincides with the distribution human researchers would generate.

\section{Computing Pipeline}
\label{sec:software}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This section describes how we generate repeated draws $\hat\theta(R,\omega)$ from $\pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$. Each run receives the same natural-language research prompt and the same dataset; only the LLM's choices vary across runs. The pipeline records the full sequence from prompt to final numeric output, so each estimate can be traced back to the underlying specification and compared with the distribution generated by human analysts.

\paragraph{Phase~1: research choices.}
\label{sec:phase1}
The LLM receives a fixed description of the research question together with a data codebook. It returns a structured specification encoding four researcher choices: sample restrictions, outcome definition, treatment definition, and an estimator command that embeds controls, fixed effects, weighting, and variance-estimation decisions. A machine-readable contract validates each specification before any code runs. Because the LLM is called with positive randomness (temperature $\tau>0$), each call can yield a different defensible set of choices.

\paragraph{Phase~2: execution.}
\label{sec:phase2}
Each validated specification is handed to a coding agent---a separate LLM session that writes an analysis script and runs it in an isolated workspace containing only the raw data. The agent may self-correct runtime errors but has no access to other runs. The pipeline checks that each run produces a point estimate, standard error, and sample size.

\paragraph{Phase~3: aggregation.}
\label{sec:phase3}
The final phase collects all validated results, classifies each specification's methodological features (model type, controls, weighting, standard-error adjustment) by parsing the estimator code, and produces the summary statistics, distributional plots, specification curves, and cross-tabulations reported in Section~\ref{sec:results}. Quality filters exclude runs with missing estimates, non-positive standard errors, or implausibly large coefficients.

\paragraph{LLM providers and access modes.}
We draw specifications from three large language model families. First, OpenAI's GPT 5.1 Codex Mini, the small context-window coding specialist of generation 5.1 from the GPT family. We use GPT 5.1 Codex Mini when running integrated research choice and execution. Second, the smaller Flash variant of Google's Gemini 3. Third, France's Mistral Devstral Medium model, which provides free API access and is unique in offering both control over model temperature and over the random seed used to generate model response. In our pipeline, however, Devstral proved materially weaker at tool-using execution and at generating executable specifications, so we use Devtral exclusively for phase 1 research choice generation. These three models differ in training data, model architecture, and coding proficiency, so pooling them broadens the support of the induced distribution of research choices. We compare their output below.

LLMs can be accessed in two ways. The first is through an \emph{application programming interface} (API)---a programmatic channel through which our pipeline sends the prompt text directly to the model's servers and receives a structured response, with no human interaction. The advantage of the API is that it requires less setup from the user (e.g., on Windows machines, setting up a Linux for Windows file system). The downside is APIs are quite costly, and ensuring robust tool calling without a proper LLM harness proved difficult. The alternative is to subscribe to a plan---such as GitHub Copilot, Claude Code Pro, or Codex Plus---to access models via the \emph{command-line interface} (CLI)---a text-based chat environment in which the model reads the prompt, proposes a specification, writes code, and executes it in a single interactive session. 

Ultimately, we preferred the CLI option because it was the most cost-effective and because it generated research choices more likely to be implementable, since those choices were immediately tested in phase 2 and adjusted if they yielded, say, a null sample. The cost advantages are substantial---for GitHub Copilot, whose subscription as of this writing is available for free to academics via GitHub Education, one can make 900 calls to GPT 5.1 Codex Mini per month.

\section{Results: a distribution of DACA employment estimates}
\label{sec:results}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Our empirical object is the distribution of $\hat\theta(R,\omega)$ induced by $\pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$ for a fixed dataset~$\omega$. This dispersion reflects variation in research choices, rather than sampling uncertainty.

\subsection{Summary statistics and distributions}

Table~\ref{tab:nhk_summary} reports summary statistics for point estimates, standard errors, and sample sizes across successful executions.

The pipeline generated 108 specifications, of which 101 produced complete numeric outputs. Seven runs attrit before that stage. Five early GPT 5.1 Codex Mini combined sessions accessed through GitHub Copilot failed validation because the session never yielded an executable set of research choices, typically after filtering to a sample with no rows or no treatment variation. Two later GPT 5.1 Codex Mini combined sessions produced a valid specification but no final results file. Phase~4 then drops 15 of the 101 successful executions: five with non-positive standard errors (three GPT 5.1 Codex Mini, two Devstral Medium) and 10 Devstral Medium runs with $|\hat\theta|>1$. The final analytic sample therefore contains $N=86$ runs.

The point-estimate distribution still centers near zero but remains wide, with median~0.003, IQR from $-0.023$ to~0.038, mean~0.011, and inverse-SE weighted mean~0.007. The retained extrema still span $-0.195$ to~0.456. Even within a single dataset and a fixed research prompt, defensible research choices produce materially different estimates.

Sample sizes vary widely (mean~165{,}521; median~100{,}852; IQR 63{,}778 to~242{,}743). Choices about year windows, age bounds, citizenship definitions, and immigration-timing rules again move the effective estimation sample by roughly an order of magnitude.

\subsection{Comparison to the benchmark multi-analyst study}

\citet{huntingtonklein2025sources} study the same causal question and report dispersion across human researchers. We design our pipeline outputs as like-for-like counterparts: effect-size distributions (Figures~\ref{fig:nhk_effects} and~\ref{fig:nhk_speccurve}), sample-size distributions (Figure~\ref{fig:sample_sizes}), and summary statistics (Table~\ref{tab:nhk_summary}).

The most directly comparable benchmark is Huntington-Klein et al.'s ``Task~1,'' the high-discretion condition in which analysts answer the same DACA--employment question while retaining broad freedom over sample construction, variable definitions, and specification choices. That is the closest analogue to our pipeline, which also leaves those choices to the AI agent. In  Table~3 of \cite{huntingtonklein2025sources}, the unweighted Task~1 distribution has mean~0.053, median~0.030, and IQR from 0.014 to 0.051 (max~0.660). Our LLM-generated distribution has mean~0.011, median~0.003, and IQR from $-0.023$ to~0.038 (max~0.456). Sample-size dispersion is also large in both settings: the benchmark median is 179{,}960 (IQR 61{,}600--356{,}787) versus our 100{,}852 (IQR 63{,}778--242{,}743).

Three caveats apply. Our distribution reflects (i)~the LLM-induced support over research choices under our prompt design, (ii)~selection into successful execution, and (iii)~the mix of LLM providers. The benchmark reports Task~1--3 objects under different constraints; our pipeline mirrors the high-discretion Task~1 setting.

Tables~\ref{tab:method_shares} and~\ref{tab:control_forms} report method shares and mean effects by control-variable functional form. In the benchmark, 82\% of submissions use linear regression, 22\% use no SE adjustment, and 25\% use sample weights. Our LLM-generated research choices still place substantial mass on linear models (65.1\% OLS or WLS), use the ACS person weight in 48.8\% of runs, and request state-clustered standard errors in 34.9\% of runs. Mean effects also move with control structure: specifications including year fixed effects average 0.022 versus $-0.007$ without them, and specifications including state fixed effects average 0.023 versus $-0.004$ without them. This is directionally consistent with the broader finding of \cite{huntingtonklein2025sources}that routine modeling choices materially affect estimates.

We view these comparisons as validation targets. Agreement in broad qualitative patterns supports stochastic replication agents as a scalable approximation to multi-analyst exercises; discrepancies reveal differences in what kinds of specifications each approach generates and can execute.

\subsection{Heterogeneity across LLM providers and workflow modes}

Table~\ref{tab:provider_comparison} stratifies point estimates by the phase~1 generator used to produce the structured specification. The filtered sample contains 38 GPT 5.1 Codex Mini runs, 18 older OpenAI Codex CLI runs, 20 Devstral Medium runs, and 10 Gemini~3~Flash runs. For that older Codex CLI subset, the archived metadata preserve the tool used to generate the specification but not the exact backend model name, so we retain the coarser OpenAI Codex CLI label rather than assigning a model retroactively.

Mean estimates remain similar across OpenAI Codex CLI, Devstral Medium, and Gemini~3~Flash (0.020, 0.021, and 0.017), while GPT 5.1 Codex Mini centers slightly below zero ($-0.001$). Dispersion differs more sharply. Devstral Medium retains the largest standard deviation (0.133), but GPT 5.1 Codex Mini has the widest interquartile range among the retained runs (0.079), reflecting broader central spread once the GitHub Copilot sessions are folded into the same label.

This heterogeneity also helps diagnose attrition. Devstral Medium remains the main source of pathological extremes: all 10 $|\hat\theta|>1$ exclusions come from Devstral. GPT 5.1 Codex Mini, by contrast, accounts for most execution-stage attrition through validation failures or missing final results. The model mix therefore matters both for the shape of the retained estimate distribution and for which specifications survive to that final sample.

Workflow architecture matters as well. Among the 49 combined single-session runs, only 39 survive to the final sample; all five validation failures and both no-results failures occur in this mode. By contrast, all 59 split-workflow runs produce complete numeric outputs, although 12 are later dropped by the phase~4 quality filters. The retained combined-session runs center slightly below zero (mean $-0.001$; IQR $[-0.047,\,0.030]$), whereas the retained split-workflow runs shift positive (mean 0.021; IQR $[-0.006,\,0.041]$). The tradeoff is intuitive: end-to-end sessions preserve more context, but separating research-choice generation from implementation yields more reliable execution.

Public coding benchmarks point in the same direction. Mistral presents Devstral as a research-preview coding agent and reports 46.8\% on SWE-bench Verified \citep{mistral2025devstral}. Google reports 78\% on the same benchmark for Gemini~3~Flash and positions it as a low-latency model for agentic coding workflows \citep{google2025gemini3flash}. OpenAI's Codex materials likewise position the Codex family as optimized for agentic software engineering, while the GPT 5.1 Codex Mini documentation explicitly describes that model as a smaller, less-capable member of the Codex family \citep{openai2025codexupgrades, openai2025gpt51codexmini}. On those external benchmarks, Devstral looks strong for an open model but not competitive with the stronger closed coding agents we use for execution, so its weaker performance in our pipeline is not especially surprising.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Standard errors measure sampling uncertainty but ignore the often-substantial variation that arises from defensible differences in how researchers construct samples, define variables, and specify models. This paper treats that variation as an estimable object. By running the same research prompt through stochastic LLM agents and executing each generated specification end-to-end, we produce an empirical distribution of estimates that reflects researcher-induced uncertainty. In the DACA--employment application, the resulting 86 estimates center near zero (mean 0.011, median 0.003) but span a wide range (IQR $[-0.023,\, 0.038]$; extrema $-0.195$ to 0.456), with patterns broadly consistent with the benchmark human-analyst study.

Our study has several limitations. First, the benchmark multi-analyst study, \cite{huntingtonklein2025sources}, may well have been included in the training data for the models we use: public project materials were available on GitHub well before our runs, including the Many Economists repository from July 2022, the public \texttt{repl\_data} repository with DACA prepared-data documentation and code from August 2023, and the public 2025 discussion paper itself \citep{nickchk2022manyeconomists, nickchk2023repldata, huntingtonklein2025sources}. We therefore interpret the exercise as measuring how current LLMs recombine public empirical templates, not as a clean test of zero prior exposure. Second, different defensible specifications can imply different estimands by changing the treated population, timing window, or treatment definition.

For empirical practice, the framework offers a diagnostic: researchers can run the pipeline on their own question and data to assess how sensitive results are to the space of defensible alternatives, flagging specifications that produce qualitatively different conclusions. The open-source pipeline makes this diagnostic reproducible.

Extending the approach in two directions would strengthen it most. First, calibrating the LLM-induced distribution to human analyst distributions---perhaps by fine-tuning on existing multi-analyst datasets---would sharpen the proxy's usefulness. Second, combining researcher-choice resampling with data resampling (e.g., bootstrap) would yield joint uncertainty measures that account for both sources of variation simultaneously.

Three design principles guide the architecture: (i)~\emph{auditability}---every run stores a full provenance chain from the research-problem description through the final numeric output; (ii)~\emph{isolation}---each execution runs in its own directory with no shared state, so that research choices are independent draws; and (iii)~\emph{portability}---resource locations resolve dynamically, allowing the pipeline to run across operating systems and LLM providers without code changes.

\subsection*{Specification contract}

Phase~1 requires the LLM to return a JSON object with four fields:
\begin{Verbatim}[fontsize=\small, frame=single]
{
  "sample_selection":        [<filter condition>, ...],
  "outcome_definition":      "<Python expression>",
  "treatment_definition":    "<Python expression>",
  "model_specification_line": "<executable Python line>"
}
\end{Verbatim}
The contract is deliberately minimal: it pins down what every set of research choices must produce (an estimator applied to an outcome, treatment, and sample) while leaving maximum freedom in how those objects are defined. The \texttt{model\_specification\_line} must be a single executable Python statement calling an estimator (e.g., \texttt{smf.ols(...).fit()}) and may embed controls, fixed effects, sample weights, and clustering choices. The contract is enforced by a JSON Schema validated before any code executes.

Phase~1 supports both API-based and interactive command-line LLM interfaces. In both cases, the system extracts the JSON object from the model response, validates it against the schema, and stores provider-agnostic metadata for auditing. A thin client layer provides a unified completion interface across providers; adding a new provider requires one adapter for authentication, retries, and metadata normalization.

\subsection*{Execution sandbox}

Phase~2 takes each validated specification and produces an executable analysis script plus numeric results. The steps are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Create an isolated workspace.} Each run receives only whitelisted input data and documentation files.
\item \textbf{Link large inputs.} Large data assets are shared across runs via platform-appropriate linking, with safe fallbacks.
\item \textbf{Invoke a coding agent.} The agent receives the structured specification, generates analysis code, and executes it with bounded self-correction for runtime errors.
\item \textbf{Validate output.} The pipeline verifies the result object exists, parses correctly, and contains numeric fields for point estimate, standard error, and sample size. Runs failing validation enter the aggregation step as failures.
\end{enumerate}
A combined mode allows a single agent session to both propose and implement a specification, useful for agents that benefit from end-to-end context.

\subsection*{Aggregation and classification}

Phase~3 merges all validated specifications and results by run identifier. The aggregator infers derived metadata from \texttt{model\_specification\_line} using regular-expression classifiers:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Model type}: OLS, WLS, Logit, or Other, from the estimator function name.
\item \textbf{Controls and fixed effects}: parsed from the formula's right-hand side, distinguishing continuous regressors from categorical (\texttt{C(...)}) terms.
\item \textbf{Sample weighting}: detected from \texttt{weights=} arguments (typically the ACS person weight \texttt{PERWT}).
\item \textbf{SE adjustment}: classified as clustered (state), heteroskedasticity-robust (HC), or none, from \texttt{cov\_type} and \texttt{cov\_kwds} arguments.
\end{itemize}
This classification operates on structured text (Python code), avoiding reliance on self-reported LLM metadata. Quality filters drop runs with missing estimates, non-positive standard errors, or $|\hat\theta|>1$.

The phase produces:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Summary statistics} (Table~\ref{tab:nhk_summary}): Unweighted and inverse-SE-weighted moments and quantiles of the point-estimate distribution, plus standard-error and sample-size distributions.
\item \textbf{Density plots} (Figures~\ref{fig:nhk_effects}--\ref{fig:sample_sizes}): Epanechnikov kernel densities with box-and-whisker overlays.
\item \textbf{Specification curve} (Figure~\ref{fig:nhk_speccurve}): Estimates ordered by magnitude with 95\% confidence intervals.
\item \textbf{Decision-share tables} (Tables~\ref{tab:method_shares}--\ref{tab:control_forms}): Cross-tabulations of estimation methods, weighting, SE adjustments, and control-variable functional forms.
\item \textbf{Provider comparison} (Table~\ref{tab:provider_comparison}): Mean, standard deviation, and interquartile range of estimates stratified by the LLM that generated the specification.
\end{itemize}
All tables and figures are generated from the same integrated analysis dataset so that manuscript objects update automatically when the pipeline is re-run.

\section{Prompt excerpt and design}
\label{app:prompt}

This appendix reproduces the key portion of the prompt used to elicit structured specifications of research choices. The original prompt also included extensive variable definitions and codebook material to support independent implementation; we omit that bulk for space and instead present the instructions that govern the required outputs and the feasible choice set.

\noindent\textit{Prompt text (governing instructions and required output).}
\begin{Verbatim}[fontsize=\small]
Research question:
Among ethnically Hispanic-Mexican Mexican-born people living in the United States,
what was the causal impact of eligibility for the Deferred Action for Childhood
Arrivals (DACA) program on the probability that the eligible person is employed
full-time, defined as usually working 35 hours per week or more?

DACA was implemented in 2012. Estimate effects on full-time employment in 2013–2016.

KEY DELIVERABLES — ONLY RETURN THE FOLLOWING USING PYTHON:
Your answer MUST contain ONLY a JSON block with the following structure:

{
  "sample_selection": ["<filter condition 1>", "<filter condition 2>", "..."],
  "outcome_definition": "<Python expression for outcome variable>",
  "treatment_definition": "<Python expression for DACA eligibility>",
  "model_specification_line": "<exact Python line calling the estimator>"
}

Rules:
- Provide implementable filters and expressions consistent with the available data.
- The model_specification_line must be a single executable Python line that:
  (i) estimates the effect of treatment_definition on outcome_definition; and
  (ii) encodes any controls, fixed effects, weights, and SE/clustering choices.
- Be explicit in sample_selection about years, age bounds, nativity/origin criteria,
  and any restrictions used to approximate eligibility rules.
- Do NOT include any commentary outside the JSON block.
\end{Verbatim}

The prompt fixes the research question and the required output contract but allows discretion over sample restrictions, functional form, controls, fixed effects, weighting, and variance estimation. This discretion is the mechanism that generates dispersion across executed estimates when the prompt is held fixed and the generator is stochastic.
