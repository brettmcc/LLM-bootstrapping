\begin{abstract}
Reported standard errors capture sampling uncertainty conditional on one set of researcher decisions, but defensible alternatives in sample construction and specification can shift estimates substantially. We use repeated, independent LLM runs as stochastic replication agents---each receiving the same prompt and dataset but generating different research choices---to measure this researcher-induced variation. Applying the method to the immigration policy--employment question studied by \citet{huntingtonklein2025sources}, 61 LLM-executed specifications yield estimates with an interquartile range of $[-0.011,\, 0.043]$, broadly matching human analyst behavior in the benchmark study. Our analysis supports using LLM agents as scalable proxies for multi-analyst designs.

\medskip
\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} Researcher degrees of freedom, replication, specification uncertainty, Large Language Models

\medskip
\noindent \textbf{JEL Codes:} C12, C15, C18
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Empirical papers routinely report standard errors that quantify sampling uncertainty. These standard errors are conditional on a set of researcher decisions: which dataset to analyze, how to define the estimation sample, how to construct outcomes and treatments, and which specification and standard-error adjustments to use. A growing body of evidence shows that researchers making different decisions can produce meaningfully different estimates from the same underlying data \citep{silberzahn2018many, huntington2021influence, breznau2022observing}. This implies an additional source of uncertainty beyond standard errors capturing sampling variation: researcher degrees of freedom.

Measuring parameter uncertainty due to researcher degrees of freedom at scale is challenging. Multi-analyst studies are expensive, requiring extensive recruitment and coordination. Pre-analysis plans and transparency reforms constrain discretion but do not measure the sensitivity of results to alternative, still-defensible research choices \citep{olken2015promises, christensen2018transparency}. Specification-curve and multiverse analyses require the original analyst to enumerate and implement the admissible alternatives \citep{simonsohn2020specification, steegen2016multiverse}.

We overcome these limitations using a novel approach: generative AI agents can perform empirical analyses at scale. We apply this approach to the DACA--employment question studied by many analysts in \citet{huntingtonklein2025sources}: how does eligibility for the Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals program affect the probability of full-time employment? Across 61 successfully executed specifications generated by three LLM providers, we obtain an interquartile range from $-0.011$ to 0.043. Sample sizes vary by an order of magnitude (IQR 57{,}840 to 262{,}673). This combination---a near-zero center with fat tails and wide sample-size heterogeneity---broadly resembles the patterns from the benchmark multi-analyst study, in which human researchers produce an IQR from 0.014 to 0.051 under comparable high-discretion conditions.

Two distinctions are essential for interpretation. First, the distribution of executed estimates is not a sampling distribution: because all runs use the same realized dataset, dispersion reflects heterogeneity in research choices, not classical sampling error. Second, different research choices can create \emph{estimand mismatch}---two defensible specifications may implicitly target different causal parameters by changing the treated population, timing window, or treatment definition \citep{lundberg2021estimand}.

We make two main contributions. First, we contribute to the researcher-degrees-of-freedom literature in economics and adjacent fields. The concern that inference is conditional on a selected specification goes back at least to \citet{leamer1983con}; formal responses treat model and specification uncertainty explicitly \citep{hoeting1999bayesian, hansen2007least}; and recent work on nonstandard errors argues that conventional standard errors omit variation generated across researchers' potential analysis paths \citep{brownlees2024nonstandard}. Empirically, multiverse, specification-curve, and many-analyst designs show that this omitted component can be large \citep{simmons2011false, gelman2013garden, steegen2016multiverse, simonsohn2020specification, silberzahn2018many, huntington2021influence, breznau2022observing, huntingtonklein2025sources}. Relative to these literatures, we provide an AI-enumerated, whole-pipeline multiverse that quantifies researcher-induced estimate dispersion at low cost.

Second, we contribute to the growing literature on LLMs in economics. Existing work studies LLMs as simulated economic agents or synthetic respondents \citep{horton2023large, brand2023gpt, wu2025survey}, as tools for economic research workflows and econometric measurement \citep{korinek2023generative, korinek2024collaborate, ludwig2025framework}, and as engines for industrial-scale scholarship \citep{novymarx2026ai}. Our contribution is to use repeated independent LLM runs as stochastic replication agents that generate research designs which can be executed without human intervention, providing a computationally tractable approximation to the distribution of human research choices at scale.

Section~\ref{sec:framework} formalizes the research-choice space and the LLM-induced distribution. Section~\ref{sec:software} documents the three-phase pipeline architecture. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports the distribution of executed estimates and compares it to the human-analyst benchmark. Section~\ref{sec:limits} discusses limitations and external validity.

\section{Framework: researcher-induced uncertainty}
\label{sec:framework}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We formalize the objects of interest in two steps. First, we define the research problem, the target estimand, and the set of defensible research choices. We then treat repeated LLM runs as a stochastic mechanism that samples from that set.

\subsection{Scientific target and defensible research choices}
\label{sec:framework_target}

A research question asks how treatment $T$ affects outcome $Y$ in a given population $U$. Define a research problem $P$ as a tuple of defensible choice sets,
\[
P = (\mathcal{U},\mathcal{Y},\mathcal{T},\mathcal{S}),
\]
where $\mathcal{U}$ is the set of defensible sample definitions, $\mathcal{Y}$ is the set of defensible outcome constructions, $\mathcal{T}$ is the set of defensible treatment constructions, and $\mathcal{S}$ is the set of defensible specifications. An element of $\mathcal{S}$ collects the control set, functional form, estimator, weighting, fixed effects, and variance-estimation choices such as clustering.

For example, in the DACA application researchers may make different defensible choices about age bounds, full-time work definitions, eligibility proxies, controls, and standard-error adjustments. Different combinations of these choices imply different research designs and, in some cases, different causal parameters.

Not every logically possible combination is defensible. A sample definition that includes only U.S. citizens would be inconsistent with the research question, whereas one that restricts attention to plausible DACA-eligible immigrants may be defensible. Similarly, a specification that controls for post-treatment variables is generally less defensible than one that conditions only on pretreatment covariates. Let $\R(P)$ denote the set of defensible research choices: combinations that are internally coherent, feasible in the data, and defensible ex ante given the stated question and standard econometric practice.

Let $\Ps(P)$ denote the underlying population distribution of observables, rich enough to construct any defensible sample restriction in $\mathcal{U}$ and any defensible outcome or treatment variable in $\mathcal{Y}$ and $\mathcal{T}$.

\begin{definition}
A defensible set of research choices is an element
\[
r = (u_r, y_r, t_r, s_r) \in \R(P) \subseteq \mathcal{U} \times \mathcal{Y} \times \mathcal{T} \times \mathcal{S}.
\]
Given a realized dataset $\omega$, implementing research choices $r$ produces an estimate $\hat\theta(r, \omega)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
When research problem $P$ is sufficiently specific to determine a single scientific object of interest, its target estimand is a scalar $\theta^\star(P)$.
\end{definition}
If the research problem is itself too ambiguous to pin down a unique benchmark object, one can instead treat $P$ as defining a set of defensible target estimands; throughout, we write $\theta^\star(P)$ for the benchmark scientific target the research problem is intended to describe.

\begin{definition}
Each defensible set of research choices $r \in \R(P)$ induces an implied estimand $\theta(r;P)$, corresponding to the causal or statistical object actually targeted by that choice of population, outcome, treatment, and specification.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
The \emph{estimand mismatch} of research choices $r$ relative to the target estimand is
\[
\delta(r;P) \equiv \theta(r;P) - \theta^\star(P).
\]
When $\delta(r;P)\neq 0$, dispersion across executed research choices mixes differences in estimation strategies with differences in targeted causal parameters.
\end{definition}

The broader causal-inference and statistics literatures distinguish the target estimand from the implied estimand and discuss mismatch between the intended question and the performed analysis \citep{blair2019declaring, choi2023question, chang2024alignment, barnard2026mismatch}. In our setting, mismatch arises when one specification defines full-time work as at least 35 hours per week while another uses 30 hours, annual weeks worked, or a different sample window; similarly, alternative DACA eligibility proxies using different age and arrival cutoffs target different treated populations. Such choices differ not only in estimation strategy but in the object being estimated.

\subsection{LLMs as replication agents}
\label{sec:framework_llm}

Fix a research problem $P$ and a temperature parameter $\tau>0$, the standard sampling parameter governing how concentrated the model's next-token probabilities are. Higher $\tau$ increases the probability of lower-ranked continuations and therefore generates more variation across runs. In our setting, $\tau>0$ allows one fixed prompt to produce multiple non-identical but problem-consistent sets of research choices.

Repeated independent runs of the same LLM on the same problem description induce a distribution over defensible research choices, $\pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$, supported on $\R(P)$. If $R \sim \pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$ and the dataset is fixed at $\omega$, the empirical object in this paper is the fixed-sample distribution of executed estimates $\hat\theta(R,\omega)$.

Why might LLMs produce plausible research choices? Their training corpora include academic articles, codebases, and empirical templates \citep{gao2020pile, chen2021codex}---the same materials from which researchers learn to define samples, write regression equations, and choose standard-error adjustments. LLM outputs can therefore be interpreted as draws from a compressed, noisy representation of documented researcher practice.

We do not assume that $\pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$ coincides with the distribution human researchers would generate. It is a computational proxy whose usefulness is empirical: it depends on whether the induced dispersion, tails, and decision shares resemble those observed in benchmark multi-analyst studies \citep{silberzahn2018many, huntington2021influence, breznau2022observing, huntingtonklein2025sources}.

\subsection{Design principles}

Three principles guide the architecture.

\paragraph{Auditability.}
The pipeline stores each run's full provenance chain: the research-problem description, the LLM's raw response, the validated JSON specification, and the numeric output. The generated Python script executes inside the sandboxed directory and remains there; a reviewer can trace any estimate back to the specification choices that produced it.

\paragraph{Isolation.}
Every Phase~2 execution runs in its own directory containing only whitelisted input files. No shared state or cross-run access exists, ensuring each set of research choices is an independent draw.

\paragraph{Portability.}
Resource locations resolve dynamically at runtime. The pipeline runs identically on Windows, macOS, and Linux, and supports both API-based and CLI-based LLM providers without code changes.

\subsection{Phase~1: structured specification generation}
\label{sec:phase1}

Phase~1 converts the natural-language description of the research problem into a machine-readable \emph{specification}---a JSON object with four required fields:
\begin{Verbatim}[fontsize=\small, frame=single]
{
  "sample_selection":        [<filter condition>, ...],
  "outcome_definition":      "<Python expression>",
  "treatment_definition":    "<Python expression>",
  "model_specification_line": "<executable Python line>"
}
\end{Verbatim}
This contract is enforced by a JSON Schema validated before any code executes. The contract is deliberately minimal: it pins down what every set of research choices must produce (an estimator applied to an outcome, treatment, and sample) while leaving maximum freedom in how those objects are defined. The \texttt{model\_specification\_line} must be a single executable Python statement calling an estimator (e.g., \texttt{smf.ols(...).fit()}) and may embed controls, fixed effects, sample weights, and clustering choices.

\paragraph{Generation modes.}
Phase~1 runs through either API-based or interactive command-line LLM interfaces. In both cases, the system extracts the JSON object from the model response, validates it against the schema, and stores provider-agnostic metadata for auditing.

\paragraph{LLM client abstraction.}
A thin client layer provides a unified completion interface across providers. Adding a new provider requires one adapter for authentication, retries, and metadata normalization.

\subsection{Phase~2: sandboxed implementation and execution}
\label{sec:phase2}

Phase~2 takes each validated specification and produces an executable analysis script plus numeric results:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Create an isolated workspace.} Each run receives only whitelisted input data and documentation files.

\item \textbf{Link large inputs.} Large data assets share across runs via platform-appropriate linking, with safe fallbacks.

\item \textbf{Invoke a coding agent.} The agent receives the structured specification, generates analysis code, and executes it with bounded self-correction for runtime errors.

\item \textbf{Validate output.} The pipeline verifies the result object exists, parses correctly, and contains numeric fields for point estimate, standard error, and sample size. Runs failing validation enter the aggregation step as failures.
\end{enumerate}

A combined mode allows a single agent session to both propose and implement a specification, useful for agents that benefit from end-to-end context.

\subsection{Phase~3: aggregation and meta-analysis}
\label{sec:phase3}

Phase~3 collects all validated specifications and results, merges them by run identifier, and produces publication-ready summaries. The aggregator infers derived metadata from \texttt{model\_specification\_line} using regular-expression classifiers:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Model type}: OLS, WLS, Logit, or Other, from the estimator function name.
\item \textbf{Controls and fixed effects}: parsed from the formula's right-hand side, distinguishing continuous regressors from categorical (\texttt{C(...)}) terms.
\item \textbf{Sample weighting}: detected from \texttt{weights=} arguments (typically the ACS person weight \texttt{PERWT}).
\item \textbf{SE adjustment}: classified as clustered (state), heteroskedasticity-robust (HC), or none, from \texttt{cov\_type} and \texttt{cov\_kwds} arguments.
\end{itemize}
This classification operates on structured text (Python code), avoiding reliance on self-reported LLM metadata.

Quality filters drop runs with missing estimates, non-positive standard errors, or $|\hat\theta|>1$. The phase then produces:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Summary statistics} (Table~\ref{tab:nhk_summary}): Unweighted and inverse-SE-weighted moments and quantiles of the point-estimate distribution, plus standard-error and sample-size distributions.
\item \textbf{Density plots} (Figures~\ref{fig:nhk_effects}--\ref{fig:sample_sizes}): Epanechnikov kernel densities with box-and-whisker overlays.
\item \textbf{Specification curve} (Figure~\ref{fig:nhk_speccurve}): Estimates ordered by magnitude with 95\% confidence intervals.
\item \textbf{Decision-share tables} (Tables~\ref{tab:method_shares}--\ref{tab:control_forms}): Cross-tabulations of estimation methods, weighting, SE adjustments, and control-variable functional forms.
\item \textbf{Provider comparison} (Table~\ref{tab:provider_comparison}): Mean, standard deviation, and interquartile range of estimates stratified by the LLM that generated the specification.
\end{itemize}
All tables and figures are generated from the same integrated analysis dataset so that manuscript objects update automatically when the pipeline is re-run.

\section{Results: a distribution of DACA employment estimates}
\label{sec:results}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Our empirical object is the distribution of $\hat\theta(R,\omega)$ induced by $\pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$ for a fixed dataset~$\omega$. This dispersion reflects variation in research choices, not sampling uncertainty.

\subsection{Summary statistics}

Table~\ref{tab:nhk_summary} reports summary statistics for point estimates, standard errors, and sample sizes across successful executions.

The pipeline generated 81 specifications; 76 produced valid estimates (93.8\%), with all five failures from Copilot~CLI combined-mode runs that did not yield parseable output. After quality filters (positive standard errors and $|\hat\theta|\le 1$), the analysis sample comprises $N=61$ runs from three LLM providers (Codex~CLI, Devstral, and Gemini~Flash; see Table~\ref{tab:provider_comparison}). The point-estimate distribution centers near zero (median~0.003) but is dispersed, with an IQR from $-0.011$ to~0.043 and pronounced tails. The unweighted mean is~0.020 and the inverse-SE weighted mean is~0.013; extrema reach $-0.195$ and~0.456. Even within a single dataset and research question, defensible research choices produce meaningfully different estimates.

Sample sizes vary widely (mean~184{,}355; median~115{,}983; IQR 57{,}840 to~262{,}673). Choices about year windows, age bounds, citizenship definitions, and immigration-timing rules produce estimation samples differing by an order of magnitude.

\subsection{Comparison to the benchmark multi-analyst study}

\citet{huntingtonklein2025sources} study the same causal question and report dispersion across human researchers. We design our pipeline outputs as like-for-like counterparts: effect-size distributions (Figures~\ref{fig:nhk_effects} and~\ref{fig:nhk_speccurve}), sample-size distributions (Figure~\ref{fig:sample_sizes}), and summary statistics (Table~\ref{tab:nhk_summary}).

The most directly comparable benchmark is ``Task~1,’’ which grants substantial researcher freedom. In the benchmark’s Table~3, the unweighted Task~1 distribution has mean~0.053, median~0.030, and IQR from 0.014 to 0.051 (max~0.660). Our LLM-generated distribution has mean~0.020, median~0.003, and IQR from $-0.011$ to~0.043 (max~0.456). Sample-size dispersion is also large in both settings: the benchmark median is 179{,}960 (IQR 61{,}600--356{,}787) versus our 115{,}983 (IQR 57{,}840--262{,}673).

Three caveats apply. Our distribution reflects (i)~the LLM-induced support over research choices under our prompt design, (ii)~selection into successful execution, and (iii)~the mix of LLM providers. The benchmark reports Task~1--3 objects under different constraints; our pipeline mirrors the high-discretion Task~1 setting.

Tables~\ref{tab:method_shares} and~\ref{tab:control_forms} report method shares and mean effects by control-variable functional form. In the benchmark, 82\% of submissions use linear regression, 22\% use no SE adjustment, and 25\% use sample weights. Our LLM-generated research choices put similar mass on linear models (68.8\% OLS or WLS), with 39.3\% using the ACS person weight and 47.5\% requesting state-clustered standard errors. Mean effects also vary with functional form: specifications that omit age yield a mean of 0.028, versus $-0.004$ with linear age controls---analogous to the benchmark’s finding that linear age controls versus age fixed effects shift the mean from 0.058 to 0.024.

We view these comparisons as validation targets. Agreement in broad qualitative patterns supports stochastic replication agents as a scalable approximation to multi-analyst exercises; discrepancies reveal differences in what kinds of specifications each approach generates and can execute.

\subsection{Heterogeneity across LLM providers}

Table~\ref{tab:provider_comparison} stratifies point estimates by the Phase~1 LLM provider. Three providers contributed independently: Codex~CLI ($N=18$), Devstral ($N=20$), and Gemini~Flash ($N=10$). The remaining 13 runs use combined Phase~1+2 sessions via Copilot~CLI; these enter all pooled analyses but are not shown separately due to the different generation mode.

The three providers produce similar mean estimates (0.020, 0.021, and 0.017). Dispersion differs more: Devstral specifications exhibit substantially larger standard deviation (0.133 vs.\ 0.020 for Codex and 0.031 for Gemini), suggesting this model explores a wider---and sometimes more extreme---region of the specification space. The IQR is more comparable across providers (0.033, 0.051, 0.049), indicating similar central mass but heavier tails for Devstral.

This heterogeneity partly reflects model capability: some Devstral specifications are defensible but numerically degenerate (e.g., treatment nearly collinear with fixed effects, or extreme magnitudes from very small effective samples). Quality filters clip at $|\hat\theta| \le 1$, but residual variation remains.

\section{Limitations and external validity}
\label{sec:limits}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Our approach is descriptive. Its interpretation depends on the relationship between the LLM-induced distribution of research choices and the choices human researchers would make. Several concerns deserve emphasis.

\paragraph{Training-data priors.}
LLMs reflect the coding conventions and modeling patterns in their training data, which affects both which research choices appear and how often. Some defensible strategies (e.g., instrumental variables, regression discontinuity) may lie effectively outside the model's support.

\paragraph{Selection into successful execution.}
Our distribution conditions on runs that pass validation. Failures from runtime errors, schema violations, or implausible outputs may correlate with the complexity or novelty of research choices, inducing selection.

\paragraph{Fixed-sample interpretation.}
All research choices operate on the same realized dataset, so the induced dispersion is not a sampling distribution. Combining researcher-choice variation with data resampling (e.g., bootstrap) is a natural extension but lies beyond the current exercise.

\paragraph{Estimand mismatch.}
Research choices can alter the implicit estimand by changing the target population, timing window, or treatment definition. We treat this as part of the object of interest and report diagnostics (sample-size dispersion, specification curve), but it complicates causal interpretation relative to a single fixed estimand.

\paragraph{Provider heterogeneity.}
Different LLM providers explore different regions of the specification space. Pooling broadens coverage but introduces variation external to any single model's induced distribution.

\paragraph{Prompt sensitivity.}
All runs use an identical prompt; we do not evaluate sensitivity to prompt wording. Small phrasing changes could shift the induced distribution, and characterizing this sensitivity is an important direction for future work.

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Standard errors measure sampling uncertainty but ignore the often-substantial variation that arises from defensible differences in how researchers construct samples, define variables, and specify models. This paper treats that variation as an estimable object. By running the same research prompt through stochastic LLM agents and executing each generated specification end-to-end, we produce an empirical distribution of estimates that reflects researcher-induced uncertainty. In the DACA--employment application, the resulting 61 estimates center near zero (mean 0.020, median 0.003) but span a wide range (IQR $[-0.011,\, 0.043]$; extrema $-0.195$ to 0.456), with patterns broadly consistent with the benchmark human-analyst study.

Important limitations remain.  The induced distribution depends on the prompt, the LLM providers, and selection into successful execution. Different research choices can also target different causal parameters (estimand mismatch), so cross-run dispersion conflates variation in estimation strategy with variation in what is being estimated. The approach does not replace multi-analyst studies; it complements them by offering scalability where recruitment is infeasible.

For empirical practice, the framework offers a diagnostic: researchers can run the pipeline on their own question and data to assess how sensitive results are to the space of defensible alternatives, flagging specifications that produce qualitatively different conclusions. The open-source pipeline makes this diagnostic reproducible.

Extending the approach in two directions would strengthen it most. First, calibrating the LLM-induced distribution to human analyst distributions---perhaps by fine-tuning on existing multi-analyst datasets---would sharpen the proxy's usefulness. Second, combining researcher-choice resampling with data resampling (e.g., bootstrap) would yield joint uncertainty measures that account for both sources of variation simultaneously.

\section{Prompt excerpt and design}
\label{app:prompt}

This appendix reproduces the key portion of the prompt used to elicit structured specifications of research choices. The original prompt also included extensive variable definitions and codebook material to support independent implementation; we omit that bulk for space and instead present the instructions that govern the required outputs and the feasible choice set.

\noindent\textit{Prompt text (governing instructions and required output).}
\begin{Verbatim}[fontsize=\small]
Research question:
Among ethnically Hispanic-Mexican Mexican-born people living in the United States,
what was the causal impact of eligibility for the Deferred Action for Childhood
Arrivals (DACA) program on the probability that the eligible person is employed
full-time, defined as usually working 35 hours per week or more?

DACA was implemented in 2012. Estimate effects on full-time employment in 2013–2016.

KEY DELIVERABLES — ONLY RETURN THE FOLLOWING USING PYTHON:
Your answer MUST contain ONLY a JSON block with the following structure:

{
  "sample_selection": ["<filter condition 1>", "<filter condition 2>", "..."],
  "outcome_definition": "<Python expression for outcome variable>",
  "treatment_definition": "<Python expression for DACA eligibility>",
  "model_specification_line": "<exact Python line calling the estimator>"
}

Rules:
- Provide implementable filters and expressions consistent with the available data.
- The model_specification_line must be a single executable Python line that:
  (i) estimates the effect of treatment_definition on outcome_definition; and
  (ii) encodes any controls, fixed effects, weights, and SE/clustering choices.
- Be explicit in sample_selection about years, age bounds, nativity/origin criteria,
  and any restrictions used to approximate eligibility rules.
- Do NOT include any commentary outside the JSON block.
\end{Verbatim}

The prompt fixes the research question and the required output contract but allows discretion over sample restrictions, functional form, controls, fixed effects, weighting, and variance estimation. This discretion is the mechanism that generates dispersion across executed estimates when the prompt is held fixed and the generator is stochastic.
