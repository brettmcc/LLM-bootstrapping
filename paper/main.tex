\begin{abstract}
Empirical papers report standard errors that quantify sampling uncertainty conditional on one set of researcher decisions, but reasonable alternatives in sample construction and specification can yield different estimates from the same data. We use repeated, independent LLM-generated workflows to measure this additional variation at scale, executing each workflow on a common dataset. In an application calibrated to the many-human analysts experiment of \citet{huntingtonklein2025sources}, the LLM-generated estimates show substantial dispersion and wide sample-size heterogeneity, with tails of economically meaningful magnitude. This variation broadly matches patterns observed in human analyst behavior, thereby supporting using LLM agents as informative proxies for human researcher variation in replication-at-scale designs.

\medskip
\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} Researcher degrees of freedom, replication, specification uncertainty, multi-analyst studies, Large Language Models

\medskip
\noindent \textbf{JEL Codes:} C12, C15, C18, C52
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Empirical papers routinely report standard errors that quantify sampling uncertainty. These standard errors are conditional on a set of researcher decisions: which dataset to analyze, how to define the estimation sample, how to construct outcomes and treatments, and which specification and standard-error adjustments to use. A growing body of evidence shows that researchers making different  decisions can produce meaningfully different estimates from the same underlying data \citep{silberzahn2018many, huntington2021influence, breznau2022observing}. This implies an additional source of uncertainty beyond standard errors capturing sampling variation: researcher degrees of freedom.

Measuring parameter uncertainty due to researcher degrees of freedom at scale is challenging. Multi-analyst studies are expensive, requiring extensive recruitment and coordination. Pre-analysis plans constrain discretion but do not measure the sensitivity of results to alternative, still-defensible workflows \citep{olken2015promises}. Specification-curve and multiverse analyses require the original analyst to enumerate and implement various alternatives \citep{simonsohn2020specification, steegen2016multiverse}.

We make three contributions. First, we provide a tractable framework for researcher-induced uncertainty by treating a \emph{research workflow} as the full mapping from a fixed prompt and raw dataset to an executed estimator, and by studying the resulting distribution of executed estimates across admissible workflows. Second, we show how large language models (LLMs) can be used as stochastic replication agents to approximate this distribution at scale: repeated independent runs from an identical prompt generate different, interpretable workflows that can be executed end-to-end on the same data. Third, we implement an auditable pipeline that makes the full chain of choices---specification, implementation, execution, and aggregation/analysis---transparent and reproducible, allowing researchers to implement the pipeline in their own empirical work.

We emphasize two distinctions that are essential for interpretation.
First, the distribution across executed workflows is different from the sampling distribution: if all runs use the same dataset, variation reflects workflow-induced heterogeneity (and its interaction with the realized sample), not classical sampling error.
Second, workflow variation can include \emph{estimand drift}: two workflows may implicitly target different causal parameters by changing the treated population, the timing window, or the treatment definition (for related discussion on explicit estimand definition, see \citet{lundberg2021estimand}). We therefore treat workflow-induced variation as an object of interest in its own right.

We apply the approach to a replication-at-scale exercise calibrated to the benchmark multi-analyst study by \citet{huntingtonklein2025sources}, which asks how eligibility for the Deferred Action on Childhood Arrivals immigration policy affects the probability of working full-time. We generate structured specifications from an LLM and execute each workflow in isolation, generating a distinct estimate. Section~\ref{sec:framework} formalizes the problem; Section~\ref{sec:software} documents the computing pipeline; Section~\ref{sec:results} reports results and comparisons; and Section~\ref{sec:limits} discusses limitations and external validity.

\paragraph{Related literature.}
This paper builds on work on researcher degrees of freedom approaches that emphasize sensitivity to defensible analytic choices \citep{simmons2011false, gelman2013garden, steegen2016multiverse, simonsohn2020specification}. Relative to analyst-enumerated multiverses, our emphasis is on an estimand and an empirical object: a distribution of executed estimates induced by an explicit distribution over workflows, where a workflow bundles sample selection, variable construction, specification, and variance estimation.

We also relate to multi-analyst studies that measure researcher-induced variation directly by recruiting analysts to study the same question on the same data \citep{silberzahn2018many, huntington2021influence, breznau2022observing}. We use these exercises as validation benchmarks for replication at scale: if a AI research agent produces broadly similar dispersion patterns and decision shares as human researchers, it can provide a scalable complement to human-intensive designs.

Finally, our approach is connected to resampling and Monte Carlo ideas, where repeated draws are used to approximate the distribution of a statistic \citep{efron1979bootstrap, andrews2000bootstrap}. Repeated LLM runs play a similar computational role for the induced workflow distribution. We also connect to emerging work on large language models as economic agents and research tools \citep{horton2023large, brand2023gpt, korinek2023generative, novymarx2026ai}.

\section{Framework: workflow-induced uncertainty}
\label{sec:framework}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Estimand and data}

Fix a prompt $P$ that states the research question and specifies the target population, outcome, and treatment. Let $\Ps(P)$ denote the corresponding population distribution over observables and potential outcomes. We use potential-outcomes notation to keep the estimand well-defined when the treatment is binary.

\begin{definition}[Prompt-level target estimand]
Let $D \in \{0,1\}$ be a treatment. Let $Y(1)$ and $Y(0)$ denote potential outcomes. The prompt-level target estimand is a scalar $\theta^\star(P)$. A canonical example is the average treatment effect (ATE)
\[
\theta^\star(P) \equiv \E_{\Ps(P)}[Y(1) - Y(0)].
\]
In applications, $P$ may define a subpopulation by conditioning on covariates; in that case $\Ps(P)$ and $\theta^\star(P)$ are understood to be restricted accordingly.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Workflow-level estimand and estimand drift]
A workflow can change the effective target population or treatment/outcome definition through its sample restrictions and variable construction. We therefore associate each workflow $r \in \R$ with a (possibly implicit) workflow-level estimand $\theta(r;P)$ and define its estimand drift relative to the prompt-level target as
\[
\delta(r;P) \equiv \theta(r;P) - \theta^\star(P).
\]
When $\delta(r;P)\neq 0$, dispersion across executed workflows mixes differences in estimation strategies with differences in targeted causal parameters.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Workflows}

We define a workflow as the full set of choices required to map data and a research question into an executed estimator.

\begin{definition}[Researcher workflow]
A workflow is an element $r \in \R$ that bundles:
\begin{itemize}
\item sample selection rules,
\item variable definitions (outcome, treatment, covariates),
\item a model specification (functional form, controls, fixed effects), and
\item an estimation and variance procedure.
\end{itemize}
Given a realized dataset $\omega$ and workflow $r$, executing the workflow produces an estimate $\hat\theta(r, \omega)$, which we interpret as an estimator for $\theta(r;P)$ when execution is successful.
\end{definition}

\subsection{A basic decomposition and the role of estimand drift}
Let $R$ denote a random workflow drawn from a distribution $\pi$ over $\R$. Let $\Omega$ denote a random sample drawn from $\Ps(P)$. The total dispersion of executed estimates across workflows and samples can be decomposed.

\begin{remark}[Law of total variance for workflow and sampling variation]
\label{prop:decomp}
Assume $\E[\hat\theta(R,\Omega)^2] < \infty$. Then
\[
\Var\big(\hat\theta(R,\Omega)\big)
= \E\big[\Var(\hat\theta(R,\Omega)\mid R)\big]
 + \Var\big(\E[\hat\theta(R,\Omega)\mid R]\big).
\]
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Interpretation]
The first term is sampling variability averaged across workflows; the second term is workflow-induced heterogeneity in the workflow-specific expected estimate. In a fixed-sample setting, variation across LLM runs describes the dispersion of $\hat\theta(R,\omega)$ conditional on the fixed realized dataset $\omega$.
\end{remark}

This standard variance decomposition does not by itself guarantee that workflow-induced heterogeneity reflects ``researcher variance holding the estimand fixed.'' A convenient bookkeeping identity is
\[
\hat\theta(r,\omega) - \theta^\star(P)
=
\underbrace{\big(\hat\theta(r,\omega) - \theta(r;P)\big)}_{\text{estimation error}}
+
\underbrace{\delta(r;P)}_{\text{estimand drift}}.
\]
If workflows change the target population or the treatment definition, then $\delta(r;P)$ may be nonzero, and dispersion across executed workflows reflects both differences in estimation strategies and differences in targeted causal parameters. In our application, we therefore report (i) the distribution of executed estimates and (ii) diagnostics based on structured specification fields (sample selection, outcome and treatment definitions) to assess comparability.

\subsection{LLMs as a workflow generator}

Fix a prompt $P$ that states the research question and defines required objects. For a given LLM and temperature parameter $\tau>0$, repeated runs of the model with identical $P$ generate a distribution over workflows, which we denote $\pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$. Our empirical object is the distribution of executed estimates induced by $\pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}$ in a fixed dataset setting.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Replication at scale with stochastic replication agents}
\label{alg:pipeline}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Prompt $P$; number of runs $K$; LLM with temperature $\tau>0$; fixed dataset $\omega$
\For{$k=1,\dots,K$}
\State Sample a workflow $r_k \sim \pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$
\State Implement and execute $r_k$ on $\omega$ in an isolated run directory
\State Record $(\hat\theta_k, \widehat{SE}_k, n_k)$
\EndFor
\State Summarize the distribution of $\{\hat\theta_k\}_{k=1}^K$ and relate it to workflow features
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Design principles}

Three principles guide the architecture.

\paragraph{Auditability.}
Each run's provenance chain is stored: the prompt text, the LLM's raw response, the validated JSON specification, and the numeric output.  The generated Python script is executed inside the sandboxed directory and retained there; a reviewer can trace any estimate back to the specification choices that produced it, though re-execution requires invoking the coding agent again.

\paragraph{Isolation.}
Every Phase~2 execution runs in its own directory containing only whitelisted input files. There is no shared state between runs and no access to other runs' outputs. This ensures that each workflow is an independent draw and eliminates cross-contamination.

\paragraph{Portability.}
Resource locations are resolved dynamically at runtime rather than hardcoded. The pipeline runs identically on Windows, macOS, and Linux (including WSL), and supports both API-based and CLI-based LLM providers without code changes.

\subsection{Phase~1: structured specification generation}
\label{sec:phase1}

Phase~1 converts the natural-language prompt into a machine-readable \emph{specification}---a JSON object with four required fields:
\begin{Verbatim}[fontsize=\small, frame=single]
{
  "sample_selection":        [<filter condition>, ...],
  "outcome_definition":      "<Python expression>",
  "treatment_definition":    "<Python expression>",
  "model_specification_line": "<executable Python line>"
}
\end{Verbatim}
This contract is enforced by a JSON Schema that the pipeline validates before any code is executed. The contract is deliberately minimal: it pins down what every workflow must produce (an estimator applied to an outcome, treatment, and sample) while leaving maximum freedom in how those objects are defined. The \texttt{model\_specification\_line} must be a single executable Python statement that calls an estimator (e.g., \texttt{smf.ols(...).fit()}) and may embed controls, fixed effects, sample weights, and clustering choices.

\paragraph{Generation modes.}
Phase~1 can run through either API-based or interactive command-line LLM interfaces, depending on the provider and deployment context. In both cases, the system extracts the structured JSON object from the model response, validates it against the schema contract, and stores provider-agnostic metadata for auditing.

\paragraph{LLM client abstraction.}
A thin client layer provides a unified completion interface across providers. Adding a new provider requires implementing one adapter to handle authentication, retries for transient errors, and normalization of usage metadata into a common schema.

\subsection{Phase~2: sandboxed implementation and execution}
\label{sec:phase2}

Phase~2 takes each validated specification and produces an executable analysis script plus numeric results. The implementation proceeds as follows for each specification:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Create an isolated run workspace.} Each run receives only whitelisted input data and documentation files needed for implementation.

\item \textbf{Manage large inputs efficiently.} Large data assets are shared across runs using platform-appropriate linking strategies, with safe fallbacks when links are unavailable.

\item \textbf{Invoke a coding agent.} The agent receives the structured specification and is instructed to generate and execute analysis code, with bounded self-correction loops for runtime errors.

\item \textbf{Validate the output.} The pipeline verifies that the structured result object exists, parses correctly, and contains numeric fields for point estimate, standard error, and sample size (with positive sample size). Runs that fail validation are recorded as failures in the aggregation step.
\end{enumerate}

A combined mode can ask a single agent session to both propose a specification and implement it. This is useful for agents that benefit from end-to-end context while preserving the same auditable artifacts.

\subsection{Phase~3: aggregation and meta-analysis}
\label{sec:phase3}

Phase~3 collects all validated specifications and execution results, merges them by run identifier into a unified analysis dataset, and then produces publication-ready summary objects. In addition to raw specification fields and numeric outputs, the aggregator infers derived metadata from \texttt{model\_specification\_line} using regular-expression classifiers:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Model type}: OLS, WLS, Logit, or Other, inferred from the estimator function name.
\item \textbf{Control variables and fixed effects}: Parsed from the formula's right-hand-side terms, distinguishing continuous regressors from categorical (\texttt{C(...)}) terms.
\item \textbf{Sample weighting}: Detected from \texttt{weights=} arguments (typically the ACS person weight \texttt{PERWT}).
\item \textbf{Standard-error adjustment}: Classified as clustered (state), heteroskedasticity-robust (HC), or none, based on \texttt{cov\_type} and \texttt{cov\_kwds} arguments.
\end{itemize}
Because this classification operates on structured text (Python code), it avoids reliance on self-reported metadata from the LLM, which may be inconsistent.

The same phase applies quality filters (dropping runs with missing estimates, non-positive standard errors, or implausibly large effect sizes $|\hat\theta|>1$) and produces publication-ready outputs:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Summary statistics} (Table~\ref{tab:nhk_summary}): Unweighted and inverse-SE-weighted moments and quantiles of the point-estimate distribution, plus standard-error and sample-size distributions.
\item \textbf{Density plots} (Figures~\ref{fig:nhk_effects}--\ref{fig:sample_sizes}): Epanechnikov kernel densities with box-and-whisker overlays.
\item \textbf{Specification curve} (Figure~\ref{fig:nhk_speccurve}): Estimates ordered by magnitude with 95\% confidence intervals.
\item \textbf{Decision-share tables} (Tables~\ref{tab:method_shares}--\ref{tab:control_forms}): Cross-tabulations of estimation methods, weighting, SE adjustments, and control-variable functional forms.
\item \textbf{Provider comparison} (Table~\ref{tab:provider_comparison}): Mean, standard deviation, and interquartile range of estimates stratified by the LLM that generated the specification.
\end{itemize}
All tables and figures are generated from the same integrated analysis dataset so that manuscript objects update automatically when the pipeline is re-run.

\section{Results: a distribution of DACA employment estimates}
\label{sec:results}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This section summarizes the distribution of executed estimates produced by the pipeline and relates it to workflow components. In the notation of Section~\ref{sec:framework}, our empirical object is the distribution of $\hat\theta(R,\omega)$ induced by $\pi_{\mathrm{LLM}}(\cdot\mid P,\tau)$ for a fixed realized dataset~$\omega$.  Throughout, we emphasize that this is a fixed-sample object: the dispersion reflects workflow variation (and its interaction with the realized dataset), not a sampling distribution.

\subsection{Summary statistics}

Table \ref{tab:nhk_summary} reports summary statistics for point estimates, standard errors, and sample sizes across successful executed workflows.

The pipeline generated 81 specifications in total; 76 produced valid estimates (93.8\%), with all five failures attributable to Copilot~CLI combined-mode runs that did not produce parseable output. After applying quality filters (positive standard errors and $|\hat\theta|\le 1$), the analysis sample comprises $N=61$ runs.  Across these 61 successful executions generated by three LLM providers (Codex~CLI, Devstral, and Gemini~Flash; see Table~\ref{tab:provider_comparison}), the point-estimate distribution is centered near zero (median~0.003) but dispersed, with an interquartile range from $-0.011$ to~0.043 and pronounced tails. The unweighted mean is~0.020 and the inverse-standard-error weighted mean is~0.013, while the extrema reach $-0.195$ and~0.456. This combination---a near-zero center with fat tails---is consistent with the premise of multi-analyst work: even within a single dataset and research question, defensible workflows can imply meaningfully different estimates.

Sample sizes vary widely across workflows (mean~184{,}355; median~115{,}983; IQR from~57{,}840 to~262{,}673), reflecting heterogeneity in sample restrictions. Choices about year windows, age bounds, citizenship definitions, and immigration-timing rules can produce estimation samples that differ by an order of magnitude.

\subsection{Like-for-like comparison to the benchmark multi-analyst paper}

The benchmark multi-analyst study by \citet{huntingtonklein2025sources} analyzes the same underlying causal question and reports descriptive tables and figures summarizing dispersion across human researchers. Our pipeline outputs are designed to provide like-for-like descriptive counterparts: distributions of reported effect sizes (Figures~\ref{fig:nhk_effects} and~\ref{fig:nhk_speccurve}), the distribution of sample sizes (Figure~\ref{fig:sample_sizes}), and summary statistics (Table~\ref{tab:nhk_summary}).

The most directly comparable benchmark objects are those for ``Task~1,'' which grants substantial researcher freedom in specification and implementation. In the benchmark study's Table~3 (Task~1), the unweighted distribution has mean~0.053, median~0.030, and an interquartile range from 0.014 to 0.051 (max~0.660). In our executed LLM workflow sample, the unweighted distribution has mean~0.020, median~0.003, and an interquartile range from $-0.011$ to~0.043 (max~0.456). Sample-size dispersion is also large in both settings: the benchmark Task~1 median sample size is 179{,}960 (25th percentile 61{,}600; 75th percentile 356{,}787), while our median is 115{,}983 (25th percentile 57{,}840; 75th percentile 262{,}673).

These comparisons should be interpreted cautiously. Our distribution is computed over successful executions in our implementation environment and therefore reflects (i)~the LLM-induced workflow support under our prompt design, (ii)~selection into what can be executed successfully, and (iii)~the mix of LLM providers used. Moreover, the benchmark reports Task~1--3 objects under different constraints; our pipeline is designed to mirror the high-discretion Task~1 setting rather than the more constrained tasks.

Beyond these core descriptive objects, we also report method and specification diagnostics that mirror the benchmark’s emphasis on how analytic decisions map into results. Table \ref{tab:method_shares} reports shares of workflows by estimation method, weighting, and variance adjustment, and Table \ref{tab:control_forms} reports how the mean effect differs across common control-variable functional forms.

Benchmark Table 4 reports that 82\% of submissions use linear regression, 22\% use no standard-error adjustment, and 25\% use sample weights (with substantial heterogeneity in clustering choices). Our executed workflows put similar mass on linear models in the broad sense (68.8\% labeled OLS or WLS), but differ in distribution across categories. On the weighting margin, 39.3\% use the ACS person weight (\texttt{PERWT}) and 47.5\% request state-clustered standard errors. In the benchmark study's Table~5, mean effects vary with control functional forms (e.g., for age: mean 0.058 with linear age controls vs.\ 0.024 with age fixed effects). Our Table~\ref{tab:control_forms} shows analogous but distinct patterns: mean effects vary across functional form and inclusion, especially for specifications that omit key controls (e.g., mean 0.028 when age is not included vs.\ $-0.004$ with linear age controls).

For transparency, we have chosen objects that mirror the benchmark paper's descriptive summaries: our Table~\ref{tab:nhk_summary} corresponds to their Task~1 distribution table; our Tables~\ref{tab:method_shares} and~\ref{tab:control_forms} correspond to their decision-share and functional-form summaries; and Figures~\ref{fig:sample_sizes},~\ref{fig:nhk_effects}, and~\ref{fig:nhk_speccurve} correspond to their sample-size distribution, effect-size distribution, and specification curve. We view these comparisons as validation targets: agreement in broad qualitative patterns supports the use of stochastic replication agents as a scalable approximation to multi-analyst exercises, while discrepancies are informative about differences in workflow support (what kinds of specifications are generated) and execution constraints (what kinds of specifications can be implemented successfully).

\subsection{Heterogeneity across LLM providers}

Table~\ref{tab:provider_comparison} stratifies the point-estimate distribution by the LLM provider used in Phase~1. Three providers contributed Phase~1 specifications independently: Codex~CLI ($N=18$), Devstral ($N=20$), and Gemini~Flash ($N=10$). The remaining 13 runs use combined Phase~1+2 sessions via Copilot~CLI, where a single agent both proposes and implements the specification; these are included in all pooled analyses but are not shown as a separate row in Table~\ref{tab:provider_comparison} due to the different generation mode.

Despite generating specifications from different model families, the three providers produce similar mean point estimates (0.020, 0.021, and~0.017, respectively). The more informative comparison is in dispersion: Devstral specifications exhibit substantially larger standard deviation (0.133 vs.\ 0.020 for Codex and 0.031 for Gemini), suggesting that this model explores a wider---and sometimes more extreme---region of the specification space. The IQR is more comparable across providers (0.033, 0.051, 0.049), indicating that the central mass of estimates is similar but that Devstral's tails are heavier.

This heterogeneity is partly driven by model capability: some Devstral-generated specifications produce conceptually reasonable but numerically degenerate workflows (e.g., point estimates near zero because the treatment indicator is nearly collinear with fixed effects, or extreme magnitudes from very small effective samples). The quality filters in Phase~3 clip these at $|\hat\theta| \le 1$, but residual variation remains.

\section{Limitations and external validity}
\label{sec:limits}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Our approach is descriptive, and its interpretation depends on the relationship between the LLM-induced workflow distribution and the set of workflows a population of human researchers would generate. Several specific concerns deserve emphasis.

\paragraph{Training-data priors.}
LLMs reflect the coding conventions and modeling patterns present in their training data. This affects which workflows are generated and how often, and can place some reasonable research designs (e.g., instrumental-variable strategies, regression discontinuity) effectively outside the model's support.

\paragraph{Selection into successful execution.}
Our empirical distribution is computed over runs that pass validation. Failures---caused by runtime errors, schema violations, or implausible outputs---may be correlated with workflow complexity or novelty, inducing selection. Future work should characterize these failures systematically.

\paragraph{Fixed-sample interpretation.}
When all workflows run on the same realized dataset, the induced dispersion is not a sampling distribution. It describes heterogeneity across workflows conditional on the realized sample. Combining workflow-induced dispersion with resampling-based inference (e.g., bootstrap) is a natural extension but beyond the scope of the current exercise.

\paragraph{Estimand drift.}
Workflows can alter the implicit estimand by changing the target population, timing window, or treatment definition. We treat this as part of the object of interest and report diagnostics (sample-size dispersion, specification curve) to help readers assess comparability, but it complicates causal interpretation relative to a single fixed estimand.

\paragraph{Provider heterogeneity.}
Different LLM providers explore different regions of the specification space (Section~\ref{sec:results}). Pooling across providers broadens coverage but introduces a source of variation that is external to any single model's induced distribution.

\paragraph{Prompt sensitivity.}
All runs use an identical prompt; we do not evaluate sensitivity to prompt wording.  Small changes in phrasing could shift the induced workflow distribution, and characterizing this sensitivity---analogous to specification-curve analysis over prompts---is an important direction for future work.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This paper develops a replication-at-scale framework that treats researcher degrees of freedom as a source of estimable uncertainty and uses stochastic LLM agents to approximate the induced distribution of executed estimates.  Three contributions emerge.

First, a basic variance decomposition identity (Remark~\ref{prop:decomp}) separates total estimation variance into conventional sampling variance and a workflow-induced component, clarifying \emph{what} replication-at-scale measures.  Second, a modular three-phase software pipeline (Section~\ref{sec:software}) operationalizes the idea: a JSON-schema contract separates analytical intent from implementation, sandboxed execution guarantees independence, and structured extraction yields a machine-readable audit trail.  The architecture is open-source and generalizable to other research questions and datasets.  Third, applying the framework to the DACA--employment question studied by \citet{huntingtonklein2025sources} with $N=61$ successful runs across multiple LLM providers reveals an induced distribution centered near zero (mean $\hat\theta = 0.020$) but with substantial dispersion (IQR $[-0.011,\, 0.043]$) and fat tails.  Provider-level heterogeneity is modest in central tendency but pronounced in dispersion, highlighting the importance of multi-provider designs.

Important caveats remain.  The induced distribution is conditional on a particular prompt, a particular set of LLM providers, and selection into successful execution---all of which shape the effective support of researcher workflows.  Workflow variation can also entail estimand drift when analytical choices redefine the implicit target population or treatment.  Using repeated runs to approximate a workflow distribution is conceptually distinct from estimating sampling uncertainty unless one also resamples data.

Future work can strengthen the approach along several dimensions: calibrating the stochastic workflow generator to human analyst distributions, enriching the schema contract to capture a wider set of economic modeling choices while constraining implementation artifacts, extending replication-at-scale designs to multiple datasets and research questions, and combining workflow resampling with data resampling to estimate joint uncertainty.

\section{Prompt excerpt and design}
\label{app:prompt}

This appendix reproduces the key portion of the prompt used to elicit structured workflow specifications. The original prompt also included extensive variable definitions and codebook material to support independent implementation; we omit that bulk for space and instead present the instructions that govern the required outputs and the feasible choice set.

\noindent\textit{Prompt text (governing instructions and required output).}
\begin{Verbatim}[fontsize=\small]
Research question:
Among ethnically Hispanic-Mexican Mexican-born people living in the United States,
what was the causal impact of eligibility for the Deferred Action for Childhood
Arrivals (DACA) program on the probability that the eligible person is employed
full-time, defined as usually working 35 hours per week or more?

DACA was implemented in 2012. Estimate effects on full-time employment in 2013–2016.

KEY DELIVERABLES — ONLY RETURN THE FOLLOWING USING PYTHON:
Your answer MUST contain ONLY a JSON block with the following structure:

{
  "sample_selection": ["<filter condition 1>", "<filter condition 2>", "..."],
  "outcome_definition": "<Python expression for outcome variable>",
  "treatment_definition": "<Python expression for DACA eligibility>",
  "model_specification_line": "<exact Python line calling the estimator>"
}

Rules:
- Provide implementable filters and expressions consistent with the available data.
- The model_specification_line must be a single executable Python line that:
  (i) estimates the effect of treatment_definition on outcome_definition; and
  (ii) encodes any controls, fixed effects, weights, and SE/clustering choices.
- Be explicit in sample_selection about years, age bounds, nativity/origin criteria,
  and any restrictions used to approximate eligibility rules.
- Do NOT include any commentary outside the JSON block.
\end{Verbatim}

The prompt fixes the research question and the required output contract but allows discretion over sample restrictions, functional form, controls, fixed effects, weighting, and variance estimation. This discretion is the mechanism that generates workflow-induced dispersion across executed estimates when the prompt is held fixed and the generator is stochastic.
