# Econometrician Review

## Overall assessment

This is an interesting paper with a real contribution in workflow design, and the draft is better than most on one key point: it explicitly acknowledges that the cross-run distribution is not a sampling distribution and that estimand mismatch is possible (`main.tex:87-89`, `128-145`, `186-187`). But as written, the paper is not yet econometrically disciplined enough for its headline claims.

The evidence currently supports a narrower statement than the manuscript makes: one particular LLM+CLI pipeline can generate a wide distribution of executable reported coefficients from the same data and prompt. It does not yet show that the distribution is a credible measure of researcher-induced uncertainty around a common causal estimand, nor that command-line LLM agents are validated scalable proxies for human multi-analyst designs.

## Major concerns

1. The paper does not maintain a common estimand, despite claiming to study one.
   The framework correctly defines a target estimand and estimand mismatch (`main.tex:128-145`), but the application appears to pool runs that answer materially different questions. If some runs use different outcome definitions, timing windows, or eligibility proxies, those are not just alternative estimators of the same estimand; they are prompt noncompliance or different estimands.

2. Identification is too weakly constrained to justify causal language.
   The manuscript repeatedly refers to the causal impact of DACA eligibility, but the implementation contract only requires a sample, outcome, treatment, and one executable model line. There is no admissibility screen ensuring that retained runs implement a coherent causal design rather than a generic association regression.

3. Selection into the final analytic sample is first-order, not a minor caveat.
   The funnel from raw sessions to archived specs to complete outputs to retained runs is substantial. More importantly, this attrition is plausibly selective: more unusual or less conventional research choices may be less likely to serialize, execute, or survive quality filters.

4. The comparison to Huntington-Klein et al. is overclaimed.
   The comparison reveals meaningful differences in center, support, sample sizes, and likely implicit estimands. Those are not cosmetic. The current draft treats broad qualitative resemblance as stronger validation than the evidence supports.

5. Precision weighting and confidence intervals are hard to justify for this object.
   Inverse-SE weighting is not obviously meaningful if runs target different estimands and use heterogeneous identification strategies. Likewise, the specification-curve confidence intervals are conditional on each run's own model and sample, not uncertainty intervals for the cross-run object of interest.

6. The theory assumes away the hardest empirical question.
   The formal setup places the LLM support on the set of defensible research choices, but the empirical pipeline documents invalid JSON, validation failures, missing results, and filtered standard errors. Whether the LLM is actually sampling from defensible choices is itself an empirical question.

## Minor concerns

- The bootstrap analogy is too loose.
- Independence is asserted more than demonstrated.
- The draft slides between defensible, archived, successful, and retained as though they were similar categories.
- Table 5 is descriptive, but some cells are too small to support much interpretation.
- The filtering rules need more justification.
- The conclusion is more assertive than the evidence warrants.

## Concrete revision suggestions

1. Reframe the estimand honestly.
   Either narrow the admissible design space so every retained run answers the same question, or explicitly redefine the object as the distribution of executable reported coefficients generated by the pipeline.

2. Add a prompt-compliance and design-compliance audit.
   Report shares of runs that satisfy the fixed outcome definition, year window, treatment construction, and minimal identification requirements.

3. Show the full selection funnel as a substantive result.
   Treat attrition as central, not incidental.

4. Stop calling the benchmark comparison a validation unless you validate.

5. Drop or demote inverse-SE weighting unless you provide a formal justification.

6. Be more disciplined in the figures.
   Make clear that per-run confidence intervals are model-conditional and not uncertainty intervals for the multiverse distribution.

7. Tighten the language on causality.

8. Make the comparison to Huntington-Klein et al. more informative by comparing inputs as well as outputs.
